## What is the Google local pack?

<AnswerBox>
The Google local pack — also called the map pack or 3-pack — is the block at the top of local search results that shows a map alongside three business listings, each with its name, star rating, review count, and key details. It appears for searches with local intent, like "plumber near me" or "roof repair [city]," and it captures the majority of clicks for those searches. Because it features only three businesses, earning a spot is one of the most competitive and valuable goals in local SEO for contractors.
</AnswerBox>

When a homeowner searches for a service, Google doesn't make them scroll. It puts a map and three businesses right at the top, above the regular blue links. Those three listings get the lion's share of attention and clicks. Everything below the pack — the organic results — fights over what's left. For a contractor, that means the pack isn't one ranking goal among many; it's *the* goal.

<Definition term="Local pack (3-pack)">
The map-and-three-business-listings module Google displays at the top of search results for queries with local intent. Each listing in the pack shows the business name, average star rating, number of reviews, category, and contact or directions options. The pack is generated by Google's local algorithm, which selects the three businesses it judges most relevant, closest, and prominent for that specific search and searcher.
</Definition>

The pack rewards the same signals as broader local ranking, but the stakes are higher because the slots are so few. A profile that's "pretty good" might rank on the local Maps results or in the organic listings below, yet still miss the three-business cutoff. Getting into the pack means beating the specific competitors who currently hold those spots.

## How does the local pack decide which businesses to show?

<AnswerBox>
The local pack is generated by the same local algorithm that drives all local ranking, weighing three factors: relevance (how well your profile matches the search), distance (how close you are to the searcher), and prominence (how established and trusted your business is). Reviews live mostly inside prominence and are the largest part of it you control. Google evaluates all three together and features the three businesses that best balance them for each individual search.
</AnswerBox>

Understanding the three-factor formula keeps your effort focused on what you can actually change:

| Local pack factor | What it means | Can you control it? | Where reviews fit |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Relevance** | How well your profile matches the query | Partly — categories, services, profile content | Review keywords reinforce what you do |
| **Distance** | How close you are to the searcher | Barely — based on location | None directly |
| **Prominence** | How established and trusted you are | Yes — heavily | Major: count, rating, recency, response rate |

The pattern is the same one that governs all of local search: distance is essentially fixed, relevance is partly tunable through your profile, and prominence is wide open — with reviews as its biggest controllable component. That's why review strategy is the highest-leverage work for the pack. ## How reviews influence appearing in the local pack

<AnswerBox>
Reviews influence the local pack through the prominence signal: more genuine reviews, a higher average rating, recent review activity, natural service keywords in review text, and a strong response rate all increase the odds Google features your business in the three-listing block. Among the factors deciding the pack, reviews are the single biggest one you can actively grow, which makes out-reviewing your local competitors the most reliable path to a spot.
</AnswerBox>

Here's how each review signal pulls its weight for the pack specifically.

### Review count

Volume tells Google your business is real, active, and used — and the pack rewards prominence heavily. The number you need is whatever beats the businesses currently holding the three slots for your searches. A profile with 120 reviews has a structural prominence advantage over one with 15, and it's far more resilient when a stray negative lands. The action is constant: earn a review from every satisfied customer, every job.

### Average rating

Inside the pack, your star rating is visible right next to your competitors' — so it's doing double duty. A higher average strengthens prominence *and* wins the click once you're shown. A believable average in the low-to-high 4s typically outperforms a suspicious-looking flawless 5.0 on both fronts. Build it through volume and quality, never by scrubbing honest criticism.

### Recency

Recency is one of the most underrated pack signals. A steady drip of fresh reviews tells Google you're active right now, which often beats a larger but stale total. A profile earning reviews every month tends to hold or climb in the pack while a dormant one slips. The action: never let the flow stall — build the request into every job close.

### Keywords in reviews

When customers naturally describe the work — "emergency AC repair in July," "great roof leak fix" — those words attach service relevance to your profile, helping Google match you to the right pack searches. You can't and must never script reviews, but high volume surfaces this language organically. The action is simply to earn lots of genuine reviews.

### Response rate

Replying to reviews signals an engaged, accountable business — exactly the prominence Google rewards — and Google has publicly encouraged it. Responses keep your profile fresh and can carry relevant terms. The action: reply to every review, positive and negative. It nudges your pack odds and reassures the prospects who find you there.

<Callout type="tip">
The fastest way to improve your pack odds is to out-earn the three businesses currently holding your slots. Look up their review counts, averages, and how recently they've been reviewed. That's your scoreboard. Beat them on count and recency — the two signals with the most headroom — and keep your average high, and you put real pressure on their position.
</Callout>

## What actually moves the needle for the map pack — vs the myths

<AnswerBox>
What actually moves the map pack is a complete, accurately categorized profile plus strong, recent, genuine reviews and a consistent NAP across the web. What doesn't work: buying reviews (a policy violation that risks suspension), stuffing keywords into your business name, expecting a single burst of reviews to flip your rank overnight, or assuming distance can be overcome with reviews alone. The pack rewards sustained, honest prominence — not shortcuts.
</AnswerBox>

The things that genuinely improve your pack position:

- **A steady flow of genuine, recent reviews** — the highest-leverage controllable signal.
- **A high average rating** built on real volume.
- **A complete, correctly categorized profile** so your reviews translate into relevance.
- **Consistent NAP** (name, address, phone) across your website and directories.
- **Replying to every review** to signal an active, trustworthy business.

The myths that waste contractors' time:

- **"Buy reviews to jump into the pack."** It's a policy violation that risks penalties or suspension — far costlier than the slow honest route.
- **"Stuff keywords in my business name."** Against guidelines, and a common cause of suspension, which removes you from the pack entirely.
- **"A burst of reviews will flip my rank tonight."** Reviews are a sustained signal; they help over time, not overnight.
- **"Enough reviews beat distance."** Distance is real. Within your genuine radius, reviews are decisive; far outside it, they can't relocate you.

When your reviews are strong but you're still not in the pack, the cause is usually a fixable profile or technical issue. The full diagnostic — verification, categories, NAP, duplicates, and more — is in [why your Google Business Profile isn't ranking](/blog/why-google-business-profile-not-ranking).

## The interplay: relevance, distance, and prominence together

<AnswerBox>
The local pack isn't won on any single factor — Google balances relevance, distance, and prominence together for each search. A business can lead on reviews yet miss the pack if it's mis-categorized (weak relevance) or far from the searcher (distance), while a closer, well-categorized competitor with slightly fewer reviews makes the cut. The winning approach is to maximize the factors you control — relevance through your profile and prominence through reviews — so you win every pack search inside your realistic radius.
</AnswerBox>

This interplay explains the most common contractor frustration: "My competitor has fewer reviews but ranks above me." It happens because the pack isn't a review-count leaderboard. That competitor may be closer to where the searches originate, more accurately categorized, or more consistent in their business information. You can't out-review your way past a distance disadvantage for a far-off search. What you *can* do is dominate the searches within your true service area by being the most relevant and most prominent option there.

So the strategy is two-sided: lock down relevance with a complete, precisely categorized profile, then pour ongoing energy into prominence through reviews. Relevance gets you eligible for the right searches; prominence wins you the slot among the businesses that are eligible. Both have to be in place.

<Callout type="warning">
Don't try to force your way into the pack by buying reviews or keyword-stuffing your business name. Both violate Google's guidelines and can trigger a suspension that pulls you out of local search entirely — the opposite of the goal. A fake one-star from a competitor is a real problem worth addressing, but the answer is targeted removal of that policy violation, not gaming the system with fake positives.
</Callout>

## Turning pack strategy into a routine

The pack rewards consistency, not bursts. Here's the routine that steadily improves your odds:

1. **Earn a review every job.** A one-tap request at job close drives count and recency — the two highest-leverage pack signals — at once.
2. **Respond to everything.** Reply to every review in a calm, professional tone, naturally referencing the service. This supports prominence, relevance, and the click.
3. **Keep the profile complete and correct.** Accurate primary and secondary categories, services, hours, and service areas keep you eligible for the right pack searches.
4. **Benchmark the businesses in your slots.** Quarterly, check the review count, average, and recency of the three currently in your pack. They're your target.
5. **Clean up NAP and duplicates.** Consistent business information across the web removes a quiet cap on your prominence.
6. **Remove only genuine violations.** If a fake or competitor review is dragging your average, address that specific policy violation — don't dilute it with fake positives.
