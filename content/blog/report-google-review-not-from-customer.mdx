Not every bad review comes from a bad job — some come from people who never hired you at all. A name you don't recognize, a complaint about work you never did, a one-star rating from a "customer" who isn't in any of your systems. These reviews violate Google's fake-engagement policy because they don't reflect a genuine experience. This guide shows contractors exactly how to prove someone was never a customer, how to report it to Google, and what evidence to attach so the review comes down.

## Can you report a review from someone who was never your customer?

<AnswerBox>
Yes. A Google review from a person who never used your service violates the fake-engagement policy, which requires reviews to reflect real customer experiences. The entire case rests on proving the absence of a relationship: no job, no invoice, no appointment, no match anywhere in your records. When you can show that cleanly, you have one of the most removable types of review there is.
</AnswerBox>

Google's review system exists to let real customers describe real experiences. When someone who never hired you posts a review, there is no experience to describe — the review is fabricated, mistaken, or malicious. That's exactly what the fake-engagement policy prohibits.

<Definition term="Fake engagement review">
A Google review that does not stem from a genuine interaction with the business — for example a review from someone who was never a customer, a fabricated complaint, or a rating intended to manipulate the business's score rather than reflect a real visit. Because there's no authentic experience behind it, the review violates Google's fake-engagement policy and is eligible for removal.
</Definition>

The good news for contractors is that this is one of the more provable violations, because the proof lives in systems you already own. You're not trying to read minds or unmask an anonymous account — you're demonstrating a documented absence. If you're still sorting out which violation category your review falls under, the diagnostic in [how to tell if a Google review violates policy](/blog/how-to-tell-if-google-review-violates-policy) maps each one to real contractor scenarios.

## How do you prove someone was never your customer?

<AnswerBox>
Prove it by searching every system that would contain a real customer — CRM, invoicing, scheduling, and payment records — for the reviewer's name, phone, address, and email. A clean miss across all of them is your evidence. Capture dated screenshots of each empty search. "This person returns no record in any of our systems" is the foundation of a non-customer report.
</AnswerBox>

A real customer leaves a trail: an estimate, a scheduled appointment, an invoice, a payment, a photo on the job ticket. A non-customer leaves nothing. Your job is to show, system by system, that the trail doesn't exist.

**Search every system, not just one.** People misremember which platform a job lives in, and a thorough search closes the "maybe it's somewhere" gap. Check:

- **Your CRM / customer database** — by name, phone, email, and address.
- **Invoicing and accounting** — any bill, estimate, or payment tied to the reviewer.
- **Scheduling / dispatch** — any appointment, job ticket, or service window.
- **Payment processor** — any transaction matching their details.
- **Communication logs** — texts, emails, or call records, if you keep them.

**Capture the misses.** For each empty search, take a dated screenshot showing the query and the "no results" outcome. These screenshots are the spine of your report.

**Check the review for an experience.** A genuine customer references something concrete — a date, a crew, a specific repair, an address. A non-customer review is usually generic ("worst company ever, total scam") because there's no real job to describe.

<Callout type="warning">
A missing record alone isn't always conclusive — a real customer might have booked under a spouse's name, a business name, or a misspelling. Before you report, run reasonable variations and cross-check the address. You want to be confident, not just hopeful, that there's genuinely no relationship. Overclaiming a non-customer case that turns out to be a real client damages your credibility for future reports.
</Callout>

## Signs a reviewer was never a real customer

Use this table to gauge how strong your non-customer case is. The more rows that apply, the cleaner the report.

| Sign | Why it indicates a non-customer | Evidence to capture |
| --- | --- | --- |
| No match in CRM, invoices, scheduling, or payments | Real customers leave records; non-customers don't | Dated "no results" screenshots from each system |
| No service details (date, crew, job, address) | A real customer references their actual experience | Screenshot highlighting the vague review |
| Describes work you don't even offer | They've confused you with someone — or invented it | Screenshot of the mismatched complaint |
| Name returns nothing, even with variations | No plausible record under any spelling or alias | Screenshots of searched variations |
| Profile mass-reviews unrelated businesses | Behavior of a fake/spam account, not a customer | Screenshot of the reviewer's profile history |
| Profile ties to a competitor | Points to conflict of interest on top of fake engagement | Screenshot of the connecting profile |

If that last row applies — the non-customer also appears to be a competitor — you may have two overlapping violations, and the conflict-of-interest angle can strengthen your case further. ## How do you report a non-customer review to Google?

<AnswerBox>
Report it from your verified Google Business Profile: open the review, select "Report review," and choose fake engagement (or fake/fraudulent content). In the description, state plainly that the reviewer is not in your records and describes no service you provided. Then track the report and escalate through Business Profile support with your "no record found" screenshots if it stalls.
</AnswerBox>

Here's the exact sequence once you've confirmed there's no customer relationship:

1. **Log in to the owner account** that manages your Google Business Profile. Reports from the verified owner carry more weight than public flags.
2. **Open the review and choose "Report review"** (a flag icon on some profiles).
3. **Select the fake-engagement category** — sometimes labeled fake or fraudulent content. This is the policy a non-customer review breaks.
4. **State the case in one clean sentence.** "This reviewer is not in any of our customer records and describes no job, date, or service we performed." Facts, not frustration.
5. **Track the report** in your Reviews dashboard and note the date you submitted.
6. **Escalate if it stalls.** No decision in about two weeks? Use Google Business Profile support (chat or the support form), restate the case, and reference your "no record found" screenshots. The full escalation path — and what to do if Google denies it — is in the [pillar guide on removing fake Google reviews](/blog/how-to-remove-fake-google-review).

<Callout type="tip">
Lead every part of your report with the record miss. "Not in our customer records" is concrete, verifiable, and exactly the kind of fact a reviewer can act on quickly. Bury it under a paragraph of how unfair the review is, and you make the decision harder. Strongest fact first, every time.
</Callout>

## What evidence should you attach?

<AnswerBox>
Attach a dated screenshot of your empty customer-record search (ideally from more than one system), a screenshot of the review showing it lacks any real service details, and the reviewer's profile if it shows mass-reviewing or competitor ties. Add a short written statement tying it together: the person is not a customer and the review describes no service you performed.
</AnswerBox>

Your evidence package for a non-customer report is short and powerful when assembled well:

- **The record miss.** Dated screenshots of empty searches across CRM, invoicing, and scheduling. This is your headline.
- **The vague review.** A screenshot showing the review contains no date, crew, job, or address — nothing only a real customer would know.
- **The reviewer's profile.** If it shows scattershot reviewing or ties to a competitor, capture it as supporting evidence.
- **A one-paragraph statement.** Plainly: who they aren't (no record), what's missing (no real experience), and the policy it violates (fake engagement).

The reason this works is that you're not asking Google to take your word about who's right — you're showing a documented absence. There was no job, so there's no genuine experience, so the review violates the policy. That's a clean, honest case.

And to be clear on the line we never cross: this only applies to people who truly were not your customers. If someone genuinely hired you and left a harsh review, that's a real customer's honest feedback — it doesn't violate policy, it can't be removed, and the right move is a calm public reply. The non-customer report is for the people who were never there at all.

A review from someone who was never your customer is, in a sense, the easiest kind to fight, because the truth is on your side and it's documented in your own systems.
