A review pops up from a name you don't recognize, describing a job you never did, in a town you don't even serve. Your gut says it's fake — a competitor, a bot, a case of mistaken identity, or someone with a grudge. The instinct is to fire back publicly: "This person was never our customer, this is a fake review, ignore it." Don't. The way you respond to a suspected fake review is read by every future prospect, and the loud public takedown almost always hurts you more than the review itself. There's a better move — actually two moves running at once — and this guide walks through both.

## Should you respond to a fake review or just report it?

<AnswerBox>
Do both. Post a short, calm public reply for the people reading your profile, and separately report the review to Google for removal. These are two different actions that solve two different problems: the reply protects your reputation with the prospects reading right now, and the report pursues taking the review down — which can take weeks and may not succeed. If you only report, you leave the review unanswered in front of every prospect for however long removal takes. If you only reply, you've let a policy-violating review stay up. You want both working in parallel.
</AnswerBox>

Here's the mistake almost every contractor makes with a suspected fake review: they treat it as one decision — reply *or* report — when it's actually two separate tracks that should run at the same time.

**The reply is public and immediate.** The moment you post it, every future customer who finds that review also sees your composed, professional response right underneath it. That's a reputation problem solved today.

**The report is private and slow.** It goes to Google, it may take days or weeks, and it only works if the review genuinely violates policy. That's a removal process started today that may pay off later.

Treat them as parallel tracks. Reply within a day or two so onlookers see you on top of it. Report immediately so the removal clock starts. Neither one cancels the other out — and crucially, the reply does nothing to weaken the report.

## Why you should never call out a fake reviewer publicly

<AnswerBox>
Publicly accusing a reviewer of being fake makes you look defensive and combative to future customers — the exact opposite of the impression you want — even when you're completely right. And if you're wrong about an obscure, one-off, or misremembered real customer, a public accusation can blow up in your face and make the review look credible by comparison. The professional move is to state calmly that you have no record of this person in your system and that you'd genuinely like to resolve any real issue, which signals confidence to onlookers without starting a fight.
</AnswerBox>

This is the heart of it. When you suspect a review is fake, your every instinct is to expose it. Resist that, for three concrete reasons.

**First, the audience can't verify your claim — they can only judge your tone.** A future customer reading "THIS IS A FAKE REVIEW FROM A COMPETITOR" has no way to know if that's true. What they *can* see is a business owner who sounds rattled and combative. The accusation doesn't read as "I'm being wronged"; it reads as "this person fights with people online."

**Second, you might be wrong.** Maybe it was a real customer who booked under a spouse's name. Maybe a job your subcontractor handled. Maybe someone you genuinely forgot. If you've publicly branded a real customer a liar, you've created a far bigger problem than the original review — and handed them a legitimate grievance.

**Third, a calm reply is more persuasive than an accusation.** "We've searched our records and don't have any account matching this name or job — we'd genuinely like to understand what happened, so please reach out directly" does more work than any accusation. It tells the reader, between the lines, *this probably isn't a real customer* — and it does it while making you look like the reasonable party.

<Callout type="warning">
Never reveal private customer details or account information in a public reply, even to "prove" someone wasn't a customer. Posting "we have no record of a John Smith at 412 Oak Street" exposes data you shouldn't, and arguing specifics in public always reads worse than staying brief. State that you have no record of them in general terms and move the conversation offline.
</Callout>

## Does replying to a fake review hurt your removal case?

<AnswerBox>
No. A calm, professional reply does not signal to Google that you accept the review, and it does not weaken or void a removal request. Google evaluates removal based on whether the review violates its policies — not on whether the business owner responded. The only replies that can cause problems are ones that reveal private customer data or descend into a public argument, and those hurt you with readers far more than with Google. A short, neutral reply is completely safe to post alongside a pending report.
</AnswerBox>

A lot of contractors hesitate to reply because they worry it will somehow "lock in" the review or tell Google they've accepted it. That's a myth. Responding and reporting live in completely separate systems. Your public reply appears on the listing; your report goes through the flagging and review-removal pipeline. One does not feed the other.

So you lose nothing by replying first and reporting second — or, better, doing both within the same hour. The removal decision will hinge entirely on whether the review breaks a Google policy, which is a question about the *review*, not about your *response*.

## Good vs. bad responses to a fake review

<AnswerBox>
A good response to a suspected fake review is short, calm, and written for future readers: it notes you have no record of the person as a customer, expresses genuine willingness to fix any real problem, and invites a direct conversation — without accusing anyone. A bad response accuses the reviewer of being fake, argues the facts, reveals private details, or sounds angry. The good version makes you look like the reasonable professional; the bad version makes the review look more credible and you look combative.
</AnswerBox>

Let's make this concrete. Same fake review, two ways to handle it.

**The bad response (don't do this):**

> "This is a 100% FAKE review. You were NEVER our customer and we have you on camera vandalizing our sign. This is clearly our competitor trying to sabotage us. Google needs to take this down NOW. We will be involving our lawyer."

Everything about this loses. It's angry, it makes accusations the reader can't verify, it references a side drama, it threatens, and it makes the contractor look like someone you'd never want to hire. Whether or not the review is fake, *this reply* is the thing that scares off prospects.

**The good response (do this):**

> "Thank you for the feedback — we take every comment seriously. After searching our records, we can't find any job or account matching this name, so we may have our wires crossed. If you worked with us in any capacity, we'd genuinely like to make it right — please reach out to us directly at [phone/email] so we can look into it."

This version is calm, it quietly signals to the reader that this likely isn't a real customer, it offers to help anyone with a real problem, and it leaves you looking like the grown-up. It does its job for the audience while you separately pursue removal.

### Do / don't: responding to a suspected fake review

| Do | Don't |
| --- | --- |
| Reply calmly within 24–48 hours so readers see you're attentive | Fire off an angry reply in the first ten minutes |
| State plainly that you have no record of them as a customer | Publicly accuse the reviewer of being "fake" or a competitor |
| Offer to resolve any genuine issue offline | Try to litigate the facts in the public thread |
| Keep it to two or three neutral sentences | Reveal customer names, addresses, or account details |
| Report the review to Google separately, in parallel | Assume the reply alone removes it — it doesn't |

The reply that wins isn't the one that proves the review is fake — it's the one that makes you look like the calmest, most reasonable contractor on the list. ## What actually makes a fake review removable?

<AnswerBox>
A review is removable only when it genuinely violates Google's policies — most commonly when it's posted by someone who was never a customer, comes from a competitor with a conflict of interest, contains prohibited content, or is spam or fake engagement. A review being harsh, unfair, or exaggerated is not enough; if it's a real customer's honest (even wrong) opinion, it stays. The deciding factor is the policy violation, not how damaging the review feels.
</AnswerBox>

This is the honest part, and it matters. "Fake" in everyday speech and "removable" under Google's rules are not the same thing. A review you *believe* is unfair, or that paints a one-sided picture of a real job, is not removable — that's a real customer's opinion, and your move there is a great reply, not a removal request.

What *is* removable is a review that breaks a specific policy:

- **From a non-customer** — someone who never hired you, which is a conflict-of-interest / fake-engagement violation.
- **From a competitor** — a clear conflict of interest.
- **Prohibited content** — slurs, threats, off-topic rants, personal attacks unrelated to service.
- **Spam or fake engagement** — bot activity, duplicate postings, coordinated attacks.

If your suspected-fake review fits one of those, you have a real removal case. If it's just a harsh review from someone who *was* a customer, it isn't fake in Google's eyes — it's negative, and the right response is the framework in our [pillar guide on responding to negative reviews](/blog/how-to-respond-to-negative-google-reviews). ## Putting it together: reply now, report in parallel

When a review you believe is fake lands on your profile, run the two-track play and don't overthink it:

1. **Screenshot it now.** Authors can edit or delete reviews; preserve the original text and date.
2. **Confirm the policy angle.** Were they ever a customer? Is it a competitor? Prohibited content? That determines whether removal is even on the table.
3. **Post a calm public reply within a day or two.** No record of them, genuine offer to help, take it offline. Write for the reader.
4. **Report it to Google separately** for removal if it genuinely violates policy — knowing the reply did nothing to weaken that case.
5. **Be patient on removal, but don't wait on the reply.** The report may take weeks; your reply is already doing its job today.
