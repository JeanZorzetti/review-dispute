## Why do most review request templates fail?

<AnswerBox>
Most templates fail because they're generic, they're too long, or they sound like they came from a marketing department. Customers read them once, feel no urgency, and move on. The templates that convert are short, mention the specific job, and feel like something a real person sent — not a bot.
</AnswerBox>

There are three death sentences for a review request:

**It's too long.** A customer who just had their roof replaced, their furnace fixed, or their main line cleared is not going to read four paragraphs. If your ask requires scrolling, you've already lost them. The entire message — intro, ask, link — should fit on one phone screen without scrolling.

**It's generic.** "We hope you're happy with our services" says nothing. It could have been sent to anyone. Customers who feel like they're on an automated list treat the message like automated mail. A single sentence referencing what you actually did — "glad we got your AC back up before the weekend" — changes the entire tone.

**It asks wrong.** Phrasing like "if you had a good experience" or "only if you're fully satisfied" is gating — and beyond the policy problem it creates, it signals to the customer that you're only interested in positive reviews, which makes the whole thing feel manipulative. Ask everyone who hired you, the same way, every time. The full compliance picture is in [is it against Google's rules to ask for reviews](/blog/is-it-against-google-rules-to-ask-for-reviews).

<Definition term="Gating">
Gating is the practice of screening customers before asking for a review — only sending review requests to customers you believe are happy, or using a satisfaction survey to filter out unhappy customers before directing satisfied ones to Google. Google prohibits gating because it produces a biased review profile. You must ask all customers the same way.
</Definition>

## What's the most effective in-person review request script?

<AnswerBox>
The most effective in-person script is one sentence, delivered at the final walkthrough after the customer has seen the finished work and expressed they're happy: "Really glad it came out great — would you mind dropping us a quick Google review? I'll text you the link right now." Then send the link on the spot. That combination — spoken ask plus immediate link — outperforms any follow-up-only approach.
</AnswerBox>

The in-person moment is your highest-converting channel. The customer is standing there, the work is visible, the emotion is at its peak, and you're right in front of them. No text or email can replicate that. Your goal is to make the ask feel natural — not rehearsed, not salesy — and to remove all friction by handing them the link immediately.

**The core script (any trade):**

> "Really glad everything came out the way you wanted. If you wouldn't mind, a quick Google review would mean a lot to us — it really helps people find us when they need the same kind of work. I'll text you the link right now so you've got it."

Then pull out your phone and text the direct link while you're still there. This matters because if the customer has to search for your business later, a meaningful percentage won't bother. A direct link that opens straight to the review input box is the difference between a 10-second task and a 2-minute friction wall.

**Trade-specific variations:**

*Roofing (after final inspection walkthrough):*
> "Really glad the storm damage is handled and everything passed inspection. If you've got a minute, a Google review helps other homeowners find us after a storm — I'll shoot you the link right now."

*HVAC (after system is running):*
> "Good to hear it's cooling down already. If you're happy with how it went, a quick review on Google would really help us out. Let me text you the link."

*Plumbing (after emergency fix):*
> "Glad we got here fast and got it sorted for you. A quick Google review goes a long way for us — I'll send you the link."

<Callout type="tip">
Get your direct Google review link from your Google Business Profile dashboard (it's a short URL that opens the review box directly). Save it in your phone's contacts or notes so you can paste it into a text in under five seconds while standing with a customer. That zero-friction handoff is what separates a 40% response rate from a 12% one.
</Callout>

For a deeper look at when exactly to deliver this ask and how to read the room, see [the best time to ask for a review](/blog/best-time-to-ask-for-a-review).

## What are the best SMS templates for review requests?

<AnswerBox>
The best SMS review request is under 160 characters, includes the customer's first name, references the job, has a clear one-line ask, and ends with a direct link. No emojis, no long thank-you preambles, no star-rating language. Short and specific outperforms long and polished every time.
</AnswerBox>

Text is the highest-ROI channel for review requests — nearly everyone reads a text within minutes. The templates below are organized by scenario. Replace `[Name]` with the customer's first name, `[job]` with what you did, and `[LINK]` with your direct Google review URL.

**Template 1 — Same-day after completion (primary ask)**
> Hi [Name], thanks for having us out today for the [job]. If everything went the way you hoped, a quick Google review would mean a lot: [LINK]. Thanks again — [Your name], [Company]

**Template 2 — Next-day follow-up (only send if no review appeared)**
> Hey [Name] — [Your name] from [Company] here. Just wanted to say thanks again for the [job] yesterday. If you get a minute, that review link: [LINK]. No pressure — appreciate you either way.

**Template 3 — Emergency fix (same day, high-gratitude moment)**
> Hi [Name], really glad we could get out there fast and get it resolved for you. If you want to share your experience on Google, here's the link: [LINK]. Appreciate your trust — [Your name]

**Template 4 — Seasonal / referral-quality customer (HVAC maintenance, roof inspection)**
> Hi [Name], good to wrap up your [annual tune-up / inspection] for the season. If you have a minute, a Google review helps other homeowners find us when they need the same thing: [LINK]. Thanks — [Company]

**Template 5 — Commercial / property manager**
> Hi [Name], thanks for trusting us with the [job] at [property address]. A quick Google review helps us grow with other property managers — here's the link if you have a moment: [LINK]. Appreciate the business.

<Callout type="warning">
Do not send more than two messages total (one same-day + one next-day follow-up). Sending three or more texts about a review starts to feel like harassment and can generate a complaint or — ironically — a negative review about being pestered. One ask, one follow-up, done.
</Callout>

## What are the best email templates for review requests?

<AnswerBox>
A good review request email has a subject line that references the job, a two-to-three sentence body with the customer's name and what you did, a clear ask, and a prominent linked button or URL. Keep the total read time under thirty seconds. Email converts at a lower rate than SMS but reaches customers who prefer it and is useful for commercial clients where texting feels too casual.
</AnswerBox>

**Email Template 1 — Residential, same-day or next-day**

*Subject: Thanks for having [Company] out today*

> Hi [Name],
>
> Really glad we could take care of the [job] for you today. If you're happy with how it came out, we'd be grateful if you took a minute to leave us a Google review — it goes a long way in helping other homeowners find us when they need the same help.
>
> **[Leave a Google Review →]** (link your review URL here)
>
> Thanks for your business.
> [Your name], [Company]

>

>
> If you have a moment, a Google review would help our business grow with other property managers in the area: [LINK]
>
> Let us know if anything else comes up.
> [Your name], [Company]

**Email Template 3 — Post-warranty check-in (30 days out)**

*Subject: Quick check-in on your [roof / HVAC system / plumbing repair]*

>

>

## Do/Don't: phrasing that makes or breaks your response rate

The exact words you use matter more than most contractors realize. Here's a quick reference table for what works and what to avoid.

| Instead of this... | Say this... | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| "If you're satisfied with our service..." | "Thanks for having us out for the [job]..." | "If" implies screening — just state the context neutrally |
| "We'd love a five-star review" | "A quick Google review would mean a lot" | Asking for stars violates Google policy and sounds needy |
| "Please leave us a positive review" | "Share your experience on Google" | "Positive" is another gating signal — it's also against policy |
| "If you don't mind taking a moment..." | "It only takes about a minute" | "If you don't mind" buries the ask in apology — be direct |
| "Our company really depends on reviews" | (drop this entirely) | Guilt-tripping makes the customer uncomfortable |
| "We hope we exceeded your expectations" | "Hope the [job] is working great for you" | Hype language feels fake — keep it grounded |
| "Click the link below to review us" | "Here's the link: [URL]" | "Below" adds unnecessary friction — put the link right there |
| "Leave a review and we'll give you 10% off your next service" | (never offer this) | Incentivized reviews violate Google policy — full stop |

<Callout type="info">
The do/don't list doubles as a quick audit for any template you already use. If you find "if you're satisfied," "five stars," or anything that sounds like an incentive, revise before your next send.
</Callout>

## How do you personalize review requests at scale?

<AnswerBox>
Personalization at scale means using the customer's name, the specific job type, and the completion date — all of which your job management software already records. You don't need to write individual messages; you need smart merge fields in your templates so every automated message reads like it was written specifically for that customer. That combination — automation plus personalization — is what makes large-volume requesting feel human.
</AnswerBox>

The personalization that moves the needle is not complicated. Customers don't need a novel about their experience — they need to see their name and a reference to what you actually did. That's enough to make the message feel human rather than broadcast.

The minimum set of merge fields to include:
- `[First name]` — the single biggest conversion lever
- `[Job type]` — "roof repair," "AC replacement," "water heater install"
- `[Completion date]` or "today" / "yesterday"
- `[Technician name]` — optional but powerful, especially for recurring customers

Most field service software (Jobber, ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro) lets you set up automated review request messages with these merge fields. You configure the template once, and every send is personalized without any manual work. The full workflow for wiring this up — including timing delays and follow-up logic — is in [automate review requests without violating policy](/blog/automate-review-requests-without-violating-policy).

<Callout type="tip">
For higher-value jobs (full roof replacement, full HVAC system install, major remodel), consider sending the request from the technician's name rather than a company alias. "Mike from ABC Roofing" feels more personal than "ABC Roofing Team" and tends to convert better on larger jobs where a real human relationship was formed.
</Callout>

## What happens after a customer leaves a review?

<AnswerBox>
Respond to every review — positive and negative. For positive reviews, a short, specific thank-you that mentions the job shows future readers that you're engaged and that you value feedback. For negative reviews, a calm, professional response that acknowledges the concern and offers to make it right is critical. How you handle both shapes how every future potential customer reads your profile.
</AnswerBox>

The review request is only half the equation. What you do after a customer leaves a review determines whether future customers trust what they're reading. A five-star review with zero response from you looks like a business that doesn't care. A five-star response that says "So glad we got your furnace running again, [Name] — thank you for trusting us on that call" builds social proof in a completely different way.
