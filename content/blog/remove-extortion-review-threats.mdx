You finish a roofing job, or you decline to do free extra work, and then the text arrives: "Pay me $500 or I'll bomb your Google with a one-star review." That is not a customer complaint. That is **review extortion**, and you have more power here than the person threatening you wants you to believe.

This guide walks contractors through exactly what extortion looks like, why it violates Google policy (and often the law), how to document the threat, and how to report it so the review comes down — without ever paying a dime.

## What is review extortion?

Review extortion is when someone threatens to post — or actually posts — a damaging review unless you give them money, free work, a refund you don't owe, or some other concession.

<Definition term="Review extortion">
The act of using the threat of a negative online review as leverage to coerce a business into paying money, providing free services, or making concessions it would not otherwise make. The reviewer's goal is the payout, not an honest account of a real experience.
</Definition>

It shows up in a few common forms for contractors:

- **The pre-emptive threat.** A prospect or customer texts "I'll leave you one star unless you knock $300 off" before you've even finished.
- **The post-job shakedown.** The job is done and paid, then weeks later a demand appears: "Refund me or the review goes up."
- **The serial extortionist.** Someone who has never hired you (sometimes a competitor, sometimes a scammer) emails demanding payment to "remove" a review they already posted.
- **The freebie squeeze.** "Do this extra repair for free or I'll tell everyone you're a ripoff."

The defining feature is the same in every case: the review is a weapon, not feedback. That distinction is exactly what makes it removable.

## Is review extortion illegal?

In most U.S. states, demanding money or services in exchange for not posting — or for removing — a damaging review can meet the legal definition of **extortion** or **coercion**. Many states classify it as a felony when the demand involves money.

We are contractors and reputation specialists, not your attorney, so treat this as orientation rather than legal advice. But the general principle is well established: using a threat of economic harm to obtain something of value is the core of extortion law. The fact that the "threat" is a Google review rather than a physical one doesn't change the legal character of the demand.

Two things make the legal picture stronger for you:

1. **Explicit demands.** "Pay me $X or the review goes up" is far cleaner evidence than vague dissatisfaction. The more explicit the quid pro quo, the clearer the case.
2. **Written records.** Texts, emails, voicemails, and DMs are durable evidence. Verbal-only threats are harder to act on, which is why documentation matters so much (more on that below).

<Callout type="warning">
Do not threaten the extortionist back, and do not promise payment to "buy time." A promise to pay can complicate your own position and can be twisted later. Keep your responses factual, brief, and free of any agreement to their terms.
</Callout>

## Why does an extortion review violate Google's policy?

Even setting the law aside, an extortion review almost always breaks Google's review policies — which is what gets it removed from your profile. Two policies usually apply.

**Conflict of interest.** Google prohibits reviews from people who have a stake in the outcome rather than an honest customer experience. Someone extorting you is, by definition, not posting a neutral account of service — they're trying to extract a payout. If they were never a real paying customer, that strengthens the conflict-of-interest angle even further. (See our guide on [reporting a review that isn't from a real customer](/blog/report-google-review-not-from-customer).)

**Fake engagement / fraudulent content.** Google prohibits reviews that misrepresent experiences or are designed to manipulate ratings. A review whose actual purpose is coercion — not describing service — fits this category.

If you're unsure which policy bucket applies, our breakdown of [how to tell if a Google review violates policy](/blog/how-to-tell-if-google-review-violates-policy) maps the categories to real contractor scenarios.

The honest caveat, and we say this on every page: this works because the review genuinely violates policy. A legitimate unhappy customer who simply leaves a harsh review is **not** extortion, and that review can't (and shouldn't) be removed. Extortion is about the *demand for payment*, not the harshness of the words.

## How do I document a review extortion threat?

Document everything before you respond — the threat itself is the single most valuable piece of evidence you have. The instant you sense a shakedown, your job is to preserve the record.

Here's the checklist:

1. **Screenshot every message.** Texts, emails, Facebook/Instagram DMs, WhatsApp, voicemails (transcribe or save the audio). Capture the full thread, not just the threatening line — context matters.
2. **Capture sender identity.** Phone number, email address, profile name, profile URL. Screenshot the contact details, not just the message body.
3. **Save timestamps.** When the threat came in versus when the review was posted. A review posted minutes after a refused demand is powerful.
4. **Preserve the review itself.** Screenshot the live Google review, including the reviewer's display name and the date. Reviews can be edited or deleted by the author, so capture it now.
5. **Note whether they were ever a customer.** Pull the invoice, contract, or job record — or confirm there is none. "Never a customer" is gold for a conflict-of-interest report.
6. **Keep a simple timeline.** A one-paragraph summary of events with dates. You'll reuse it for Google, for police, and for an attorney if it comes to that.

<Callout type="tip">
Back up screenshots somewhere outside your phone — email them to yourself or drop them in cloud storage. Phones get lost and break; your evidence shouldn't disappear with them.
</Callout>

## Review extortion: what to do and what not to do

When the adrenaline hits, contractors make predictable mistakes. Here's the clean version of what helps and what hurts.

| Do | Don't |
| --- | --- |
| Screenshot every message before replying | Reply in anger or fire off threats of your own |
| Keep responses factual, short, and calm | Promise to pay or hint that you might |
| Save the threat, the review, and timestamps | Delete messages or "block and forget" — you lose evidence |
| Report the review to Google with proof attached | Assume Google will catch it on its own |
| Note whether they were ever a real customer | Admit to any wrongdoing in writing |
| Consult police/an attorney for explicit money demands | Pay to "make it go away" |
| Respond publicly only to state the facts (not a customer, retaliatory) | Argue line-by-line in the public reply or reveal private details |

## How do I report an extortion review to Google?

Report the review through your Google Business Profile and flag the specific policy it violates — then reinforce it with the evidence you collected. Here is the practical sequence.

1. **Flag it in the dashboard.** Open Google Business Profile, find the review, and use the report/flag option. Choose the closest violation category — typically conflict of interest or fake/fraudulent content.
2. **Be specific, not emotional.** When you get a text field, state plainly: "This reviewer demanded payment in exchange for not posting/removing this review. The reviewer was never a paying customer." Facts, dates, no rant.
3. **Use the Business Profile support channels.** If the in-dashboard flag stalls, escalate through Google Business Profile support (chat or the help community). Reference your report and the policy violated.
4. **Attach or reference your documentation.** Where Google allows uploads or follow-up, provide the threat screenshots and the timeline. Evidence of the explicit demand is what separates an extortion case from an ordinary dispute.
5. **Be patient and persistent.** Removal timelines vary — some reviews come down in days, others take repeated escalation. Our guide on [how long Google review removal takes](/blog/how-long-google-review-removal-takes) sets realistic expectations, and the [pillar guide on removing fake reviews](/blog/how-to-remove-fake-google-review) covers the full reporting flow.

A well-documented extortion report is one of the stronger removal cases there is, precisely because the violation is so clear.

## Should I involve the police or an attorney?

Yes, when the demand is explicit. An overt "pay or else" message crosses from reputation problem into potential crime, and contractors are often surprised how seriously local agencies take a clean, documented case.

When to escalate beyond Google:

- **The demand is explicit and in writing.** "$500 or the review stays up" is the kind of evidence police and prosecutors can act on.
- **It's repeated or escalating.** A one-off bad review is one thing; sustained threats are another.
- **There's a real-world component.** Threats that go beyond reviews (showing up at the job site, threatening employees) are an immediate police matter.

Practical steps:

1. **File a police report.** Bring your screenshots and timeline. Even if they don't pursue it immediately, the report creates an official record — and can support your Google escalation.
2. **Send a cease-and-desist.** An attorney can issue a letter that often ends the behavior on its own. Many contractors find a single lawyer's letter is cheaper and faster than a prolonged fight.
3. **Consult on small-claims or civil options.** Depending on losses and your state, there may be civil remedies. An attorney can tell you whether it's worth pursuing.

<Callout type="warning">
Don't let embarrassment stop you from reporting. Extortionists count on contractors feeling that involving police is "overkill" or bad for business. Reporting protects you and creates the paper trail that gets reviews removed and repeat offenders stopped.
</Callout>

## What if I already paid — can I still get the review removed?

You may still be able to get it removed, and you should report it regardless. Paying doesn't legitimize the review; if anything, a payment followed by a review (or a payment to remove one) is further evidence of the extortion scheme.

- **Stop any further payments immediately.** One payment rarely satisfies an extortionist.
- **Document the payment.** Bank records, receipts, the message demanding it. This is now part of your evidence.
- **Report the review and the scheme to Google** using the same conflict-of-interest/fraud framing.
- **Talk to police or an attorney.** Once money has changed hands under a threat, the legal picture is often clearer, not murkier.

The fact that you paid under pressure doesn't make the review a legitimate customer experience. It's still a policy violation, and it's still removable.

## The bottom line

Review extortion preys on the fear that one bad review will sink your business. It can hurt — but the threat is also the extortionist's biggest mistake, because it hands you the evidence to get the review removed and, when warranted, to put the person on a police record.

Document first. Stay calm. Report the violation to Google. Escalate to authorities for explicit demands. And never, ever pay.
