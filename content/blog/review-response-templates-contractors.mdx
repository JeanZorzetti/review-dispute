You don't have time to draft a thoughtful review response from scratch every time, and you shouldn't have to. What you need is a set of proven structures you can adapt in two minutes — calm, professional, and on-message no matter how you're feeling that day. This guide gives you copy-paste-adaptable templates for the situations contractors actually face: the fair negative where you genuinely messed up, the unfair-but-real review, the price complaint, the delay complaint, the review you suspect is fake, and the happy customer. Each one comes with a GOOD and a BAD example so you can see exactly what works and what backfires.

<KeyTakeaways>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Templates are scaffolding, not scripts</strong> — always add the name and a job-specific detail.</li>
    <li><strong>One structure per situation:</strong> fair negative, unfair review, price, delay, fake, positive.</li>
    <li><strong>Keep it to 3–4 sentences.</strong> Long replies read as defensive; short replies read as confident.</li>
    <li><strong>Never paste the same reply twice</strong> — verbatim repeats look automated and lazy.</li>
  </ul>
</KeyTakeaways>

## How do you use a review response template without sounding robotic?

<AnswerBox>
Use the template for the structure — the calm tone, the acknowledge-respond-offline flow — and then customize three things every time: the customer's name, a specific detail about their job, and the exact issue they raised. The skeleton stays the same; the specifics make it real. A template pasted verbatim across multiple reviews reads as automated and tells future customers you don't actually care, which undoes the whole point.
</AnswerBox>

The fear that templates sound robotic is legitimate — but only if you use them wrong. The robotic effect comes from posting the identical text repeatedly, not from using a structure. Think of a template the way you'd think of a job estimate format: the headings are consistent, but the numbers and scope are unique to each customer. Same here. The four-part framework from [how to respond to negative Google reviews](/blog/how-to-respond-to-negative-google-reviews) — acknowledge, apologize where warranted, take it offline, stay professional — is the spine of every template below. What changes is the flesh.

Before you paste anything, also know what to avoid. The fastest way to ruin a good template is to add the wrong thing: a defensive jab, a private detail, an admission of legal liability. Read [what not to say when replying to a bad review](/blog/what-not-to-say-replying-bad-review) so you don't paste yourself into a worse spot.

<Callout type="tip">
Replace every bracketed placeholder before you post — [Name], [specific job], [phone/email]. The number-one tell of a lazy contractor is a public reply that still reads "Hi [Name], we're sorry about your [issue]." Proofread once, out loud, before you hit publish.
</Callout>

## Template 1: The fair negative (you genuinely dropped the ball)

<AnswerBox>
For a fair negative review, own it cleanly: acknowledge the specific failing, apologize without excuses, and offer to make it right offline. A confident, no-excuses apology reads as professionalism to future customers — far better than defending yourself. Keep it short and resist the urge to explain why it happened in detail; the customer wants accountability, not your scheduling backstory.
</AnswerBox>

When you actually messed up — the crew was late, the cleanup was sloppy, a callback got missed — the worst thing you can do is get defensive. Own it.

**GOOD:**
> "Hi James, you're right that we ran two days behind on your fence install, and that's on us — no excuses. I've already adjusted how we schedule the crew so it doesn't happen again. I'd genuinely like to make this right; please call me directly at [phone]. — Marcus, [Company]"

**BAD:**
> "We're sorry you feel that way, but the weather and a supplier issue were out of our control, and we did finish the job. We can't please everyone."

The good version owns the failing, shows a fix, and invites resolution. The bad version blames external factors, minimizes the customer's experience ("you feel that way"), and ends with a shrug that every future prospect will read as "this contractor argues with unhappy customers."

| Beat | Do | Don't |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Open | Name + acknowledge the specific issue | "We're sorry you feel that way" |
| Middle | Brief, clean apology + one line on the fix | A paragraph of excuses |
| Close | Direct offline invitation with your number | "We did everything we could" |

## Template 2: The unfair but real review (one-sided or exaggerated)

<AnswerBox>
For an unfair but genuine review, stay calm and state your side exactly once, briefly and neutrally, then take it offline — do not argue point by point. You won't win a public debate, and a long rebuttal makes the complaint look bigger. One measured sentence of context, an invitation to talk, and you stop. Future readers will side with the professional, not the person who's technically right.
</AnswerBox>

Some reviews are from real customers but feel deeply unfair — exaggerated, one-sided, or leaving out the part where you bent over backwards. The temptation to set the record straight is huge. Resist it past one sentence.

**GOOD:**
> "Hi Dana, thank you for the feedback — I see this differently than you do, but I take it seriously. Our records show the extra charges were approved in writing before we proceeded, though I'd genuinely like to understand your concern. Please call me at [phone] so we can talk it through. — Marcus, [Company]"

**BAD:**
> "This is completely false. You signed the change order on March 3rd, you approved every charge by text, and you never once complained until now. We have all the documentation."

The good version states the key fact once, stays respectful, and opens a door. The bad version "wins" the argument and loses every reader — it's combative, it airs the dispute publicly, and it makes you look like someone who keeps receipts to fight customers. For more on this exact failure mode, see [what not to say when replying to a bad review](/blog/what-not-to-say-replying-bad-review).

## Template 3: The price complaint

<AnswerBox>
For a price complaint, acknowledge the concern, briefly explain the value behind your pricing, and never get defensive or argue that they got a fair deal. Future customers reading a price review are mostly checking whether you nickel-and-dime people or stand behind your work. A calm, confident explanation of what's included reassures them; a defensive justification scares them off.
</AnswerBox>

Price reviews are really value reviews to everyone reading. Don't argue the dollar amount — explain what it bought.

**GOOD:**
> "Hi Robert, I appreciate the feedback on pricing. Our quotes include licensed, insured crews, permitted work, and a full warranty, which can run higher than unlicensed alternatives — but it's what protects you if anything goes wrong. I'm happy to walk you through exactly what was included; call me at [phone]. — Marcus, [Company]"

**BAD:**
> "Our prices are competitive and you agreed to them before we started. You get what you pay for. If you wanted cheap, you should have hired someone else."

The good version reframes price as value and stays warm. The bad version is condescending ("you get what you pay for"), combative, and tells every budget-conscious prospect they'll be talked down to.

## Template 4: The delay complaint

<AnswerBox>
For a delay complaint, apologize for the timing, give the honest reason in one brief line without making it an excuse, and show what you've changed. Customers forgive delays they understand far more than ones they feel were ignored. Pair a genuine apology with a concrete improvement and an offer to make it right, and the response works in your favor.
</AnswerBox>

Delays happen in this trade. The reply that lands is honest about the why, but doesn't hide behind it.

**GOOD:**
> "Hi Priya, you're right that the project ran longer than we estimated — a permit delay set us back, but communicating that to you was our job and we fell short. We've since added a dedicated project updates check-in so customers always know where things stand. I'd like to make this up to you; please reach me at [phone]. — Marcus, [Company]"

**BAD:**
> "Delays are normal in construction. The permit office is slow, suppliers are backed up, and there's nothing we can do about any of it. The job got done."

The good version apologizes for the *communication* failure (the part that was in your control), explains briefly, and shows a fix. The bad version treats the delay as the customer's problem to accept and signals that future customers should expect to be left in the dark.

## Template 5: The review you suspect is fake

<AnswerBox>
For a review you suspect is fake, post a short, calm, neutral reply that notes you can't locate the person in your customer records and invites anyone with a genuine concern to contact you — without attacking the reviewer. This signals to other readers it may not be a real customer while keeping you professional. Then pursue removal separately, because replying never removes a review.
</AnswerBox>

You think the review is fake — a competitor, a non-customer, a mistaken identity. You can't prove it in the reply, and attacking the poster looks bad. Stay measured and let the calm tone do the work.

**GOOD:**
> "Hi, we take every review seriously, but we're unable to locate you in our customer records and don't have a record of providing this service. If we've genuinely worked together, please reach out at [phone] so we can make it right. — Marcus, [Company]"

**BAD:**
> "You were NEVER our customer. This is a fake review, probably from a competitor, and we're reporting you to Google. People like you should be ashamed."

The good version plants reasonable doubt for readers and stays gracious. The bad version is an angry accusation that makes *you* look unhinged, even if you're right — and it does nothing to remove the review. Replying and removal are separate actions. For the full approach, see [how to respond to a fake review](/blog/how-to-respond-to-fake-review).

<Callout type="warning">
Posting a reply does not remove a review. If a review genuinely violates Google's policy — a non-customer, a competitor, or prohibited content like threats or slurs — you must report it separately through your Business Profile. A great public reply manages the reader's impression; only a successful policy report takes the review down.
</Callout>

## Template 6: The happy customer (don't skip these)

<AnswerBox>
For positive reviews, post a quick, warm, specific thank-you that mentions what they praised. It encourages more positive reviews, shows future customers you're engaged, and keeps your profile looking active and cared-for. Keep it brief and avoid pasting the identical 'Thank you!' on every one — vary it and reference the specific job to keep it genuine.
</AnswerBox>

Responding to good reviews is the easiest reputation win there is, and most contractors skip it. A short, specific thank-you compounds over time.

**GOOD:**
> "Thank you, Maria! It was a pleasure handling the roof replacement on your place, and the crew really enjoyed working with you. We're always here if you need anything down the road. — Marcus, [Company]"

**BAD:**
> "Thanks for the review!" (pasted identically on all 40 positive reviews)

The good version is warm and specific. The bad version is fine once — but pasted everywhere, it signals automation and undercuts the authenticity of your whole profile.

## Quick-reference: situation to template

<AnswerBox>
Match the situation to the right template: fair negative → own it and fix it; unfair but real → one calm sentence then offline; price → explain value; delay → apologize for communication and show a fix; suspected fake → neutral records note plus separate removal; positive → specific warm thank-you. Every template shares the same structure — acknowledge, respond, take it offline, stay professional — adjusted for the situation.
</AnswerBox>

| Situation | Lead with | Key move | Avoid |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Fair negative | Ownership | Apologize + show the fix | Excuses, blaming weather/suppliers |
| Unfair but real | Calm acknowledgment | One fact, then offline | Point-by-point rebuttal |
| Price complaint | Appreciation | Reframe as value | "You get what you pay for" |
| Delay complaint | Apology for timing | Own the communication gap | "Delays are normal" |
| Suspected fake | Neutral records note | Reply + separate removal report | Public accusations |
| Positive | Specific thanks | Reference the actual job | Identical pasted reply |
