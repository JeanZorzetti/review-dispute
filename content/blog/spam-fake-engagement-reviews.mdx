Two of the five removable review categories blur together in practice, which is why contractors keep missing them. Spam and fake engagement both come down to the same root problem: the review doesn't reflect a real experience with your business. One advertises, duplicates, or games the system; the other comes from someone who was never your customer at all. They overlap so often — a bot account dropping a promotional link is both — that it's worth treating them as one diagnostic family. This guide breaks down every flavor of spam and fake engagement a home-services business actually runs into, the tell-tale signs of each, and the exact evidence that gets them removed.

## What's the difference between a spam review and a fake engagement review?

<AnswerBox>
A spam review is content posted to advertise, manipulate, or game your listing — ads with links, duplicated text, bot gibberish, or reviews traded and paid for. A fake engagement review is any rating from someone who never actually used your business. They overlap constantly: a bot account dropping a promo link is both spam and fake engagement. Both violate Google's policy because neither reflects a genuine customer experience, and both are eligible for removal.
</AnswerBox>

The reason these two categories sit together in Google's [contributed-content policy](/blog/google-review-policy-explained) is that they fail the same basic test. A review is supposed to describe a real transaction between a real customer and your business. Spam fails that test by being about something else entirely — an ad, a duplicate, a bot's output. Fake engagement fails it by coming from someone who never transacted with you at all. In either case, there's no genuine experience underneath the words, which is precisely what makes the review removable.

<Definition term="Fake engagement">
Any review or rating from a person who never used the business — fabricated reviews, retaliatory ratings from non-customers, and coordinated bursts of fake stars designed to move a score. The defining test is the absence of a real transaction: no job, no invoice, no record. It does not depend on whether the review is positive or negative.
</Definition>

<Definition term="Spam">
Contributed content that exists to advertise, manipulate, or game the platform rather than describe an experience — promotional links, phone numbers, duplicated reviews across listings, bot-generated gibberish, keyword stuffing, and reviews exchanged or paid for. Spam violates policy regardless of who posts it or what rating it carries.
</Definition>

## The spam signals: what fake and junk reviews look like

Spam wears several disguises on a contractor's profile. Here's each one, what it looks like in the field, and how to spot it before you waste a report.

### Bot reviews and gibberish

<AnswerBox>
Bot reviews are automated posts that don't describe a real experience — random characters, machine-translated nonsense, repeated boilerplate, or strings of keywords. They're often deposited in bursts across many unrelated businesses by the same account. Google's automation catches a lot of these proactively, but single bot reviews routinely slip through and need to be reported manually.
</AnswerBox>

Bot reviews are the most obviously fake, which makes them easy to identify but sometimes oddly persistent. You'll see a one- or five-star rating attached to text that no human customer would write: "Good good service nice," strings of random letters, or text that's clearly auto-translated from another language and has nothing to do with roofing, HVAC, or plumbing.

**Tell-tale signs:**

- Text that's grammatically broken in a machine way, not a human way.
- The reviewer's profile shows dozens of identical or near-identical posts across unrelated businesses and cities.
- A rating with no plausible connection to your service.

**Evidence to gather:** A screenshot of the review, plus the reviewer's profile showing the mass-posting pattern. The profile history is usually the clincher — a normal customer doesn't review forty businesses in three countries in one afternoon.

### Reviews with promotional links or contact info

<AnswerBox>
A review that contains a URL, promo code, or phone number for another business isn't feedback — it's an ad, and ads in reviews violate Google's spam policy. The reviewer is using your listing as free advertising space. These are among the cleanest cases to report because the violation is visible right in the text.
</AnswerBox>

This is one of the easiest spam types to win on, because the evidence is the review itself. Someone posts "Great work, also check out cheapviagra-dot-com" or "For better prices call 555-0199" — they're hijacking your profile to advertise, and the promotional content has nothing to do with an experience with you.

**Tell-tale signs:**

- A link, web address, promo code, or unrelated phone number embedded in the text.
- Language that reads like marketing copy rather than a customer account.
- A call to action directing readers somewhere other than your business.

**Evidence to gather:** A dated screenshot of the review with the promotional content visible. That's usually the entire case — point to the ad and name the spam policy.

### Duplicate and repetitive content

<AnswerBox>
A duplicate review is the same text posted on your listing and on other businesses' listings, or the same reviewer posting near-identical content repeatedly. Duplication signals the review isn't a genuine, specific account of your service — it's copy-pasted filler used to manipulate or pad ratings, which violates the spam policy.
</AnswerBox>

Duplicates show up in two ways. The first is one reviewer posting the same generic blurb on you and a string of other businesses — "Terrible experience, avoid at all costs" pasted everywhere. The second is the same review text appearing under different names, a sign of coordinated fake activity.

**Tell-tale signs:**

- Identical or near-identical text appears on other businesses' profiles.
- The same complaint, word for word, shows up more than once.
- Generic language that could apply to any business because it describes none specifically.

**Evidence to gather:** Screenshots of the duplicate text appearing on other listings, side by side with the review on yours. The match is the proof.

### Review swaps and reciprocal arrangements

<AnswerBox>
A review swap is an arrangement where two businesses agree to post positive reviews of each other, or where reviewers trade reviews to inflate ratings. Because neither review reflects a real customer experience, swaps violate Google's policy — they're a manipulation scheme dressed up as feedback, and they're removable even when the text is positive.
</AnswerBox>

Swaps are less visible than ads or bots because they look like normal reviews. They matter to contractors mostly when a competitor uses them to inflate their own rating, but you may also encounter a reviewer who's part of a swap ring leaving you a retaliatory one-star after you declined to participate. The violation isn't the sentiment — it's that the review is part of a reciprocal manipulation rather than a genuine experience.

**Tell-tale signs:**

- Two businesses with no customer relationship reviewing each other glowingly.
- Reviewer profiles that consistently appear on the same cluster of businesses.
- A coordinated pattern of mutual five-stars among a small group of accounts.

**Evidence to gather:** Profile-history screenshots showing the reciprocal pattern, and notes documenting the lack of any real customer relationship. These cases lean heavily on the relationship between the accounts, which is the same logic that drives [conflict-of-interest reviews](/blog/conflict-of-interest-reviews).

### Incentivized and paid reviews

<AnswerBox>
An incentivized review is one given in exchange for a discount, gift card, free work, or any other reward — and a paid review is one purchased outright. Both violate Google's policy because the compensation biases the content. Asking happy customers for an honest review is allowed; offering them anything in return for it is not, and the resulting reviews are removable.
</AnswerBox>

This one trips up well-meaning contractors as much as bad actors. Offering "$25 off your next service if you leave us a review" feels like good marketing, but it's a policy violation — and it's the most common way honest businesses accidentally break the rules. On the other side, a competitor buying a batch of fake five-stars, or a customer who was bribed to leave you a one-star, both fall here too.

**Tell-tale signs:**

- The review or surrounding context references a reward, discount, or payment for reviewing.
- A sudden burst of suspiciously uniform positive reviews on a competitor's profile.
- A reviewer who admits, in the text, to being compensated.

**Evidence to gather:** Any documentation of the incentive — screenshots of the offer, messages referencing payment, or the review text admitting compensation.

<Callout type="warning">
Never offer customers a discount, gift card, or any reward for leaving a review — even a positive one. Incentivized reviews violate Google's policy and can get your own legitimate reviews stripped or your profile flagged. The safe play is to ask happy customers for an honest review with no strings attached.
</Callout>

## Fake engagement: reviews from people who never used your business

<AnswerBox>
Fake engagement is the most common removable category for home-services businesses. It covers any rating from a non-customer — fabricated reviews, retaliation from people you never served, and coordinated bursts of fake stars. The test is whether the person is in your customer records: no job, no invoice, no match in your system means you likely have a fake-engagement violation.
</AnswerBox>

If spam is about *what* the review contains, fake engagement is about *who* posted it. The defining fact is the absence of a real transaction. For contractors, this is the workhorse category, because it hinges on something you control completely: your own records.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- A one-star from a name you've never heard of, with no address, job date, or service details.
- A review describing work you don't even offer.
- A cluster of low ratings in a single day, often with no text.
- A profile that has reviewed dozens of unrelated businesses across different cities in a short window.

**Tell-tale signs:**

- No identifying details a real customer would know — the crew's name, the date, the specific job.
- The reviewer's name and contact info return nothing in your CRM, invoicing, and job calendar.
- Vague, generic language with nothing concrete to verify.

**Evidence to gather:** A dated screenshot of an empty search in your customer system ("no record found"), the reviewer's profile showing scattershot behavior, and a screenshot of the review with its date. The headline of your report is one sentence: *this person is not in our customer records.*

<Callout type="warning">
A bare one-star rating with no comment is NOT automatically fake. Real customers leave star-only ratings all the time, even unhappy ones. The lack of detail is a reason to investigate — not proof. You only have a fake-engagement case once your records confirm the person was never a customer.
</Callout>

## How to report spam and fake engagement (and what NOT to report)

Here's the workflow once you've identified the violation:

1. **Confirm the category and the evidence.** Spam needs a visible link, duplicate, or incentive; fake engagement needs a "no record found" search. If you don't have the evidence, you don't yet have a case.
2. **Report from your verified Business Profile.** Owner reports carry more weight than anonymous flags because they come from an accountable source with access to records.
3. **Lead with one undeniable fact.** "This review contains an advertisement for another business," or "This reviewer is not in our customer records." Name the exact policy.
4. **Attach or reference the evidence.** A screenshot of the ad, the duplicate, or the empty CRM search makes the violation impossible to miss.
5. **Re-report with stronger proof if denied, then escalate.** Many removals land on the second or third attempt, not the first — automation defaults to leaving content up.

What you should *not* report as spam or fake engagement: a real customer's honest, even harsh, review. If the person is in your records and describing a genuine experience, no amount of dislike makes it spam. Reporting legitimate reviews wastes your credibility and can backfire.
