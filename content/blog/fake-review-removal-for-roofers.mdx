## Why are fake reviews such a big deal for roofers specifically?

<AnswerBox>
Fake reviews hit roofers harder than almost any other trade because of two factors that collide: enormous ticket size and storm-driven competition. A roof is a $10,000–$30,000 once-in-a-decade purchase, so homeowners research carefully and weigh every review heavily — meaning one fake can scare off multiple high-value prospects. And every major storm floods the local market with out-of-town storm chasers and ramps up competition, giving competitors and non-customers both the motive and the cover to plant fake reviews exactly when your visibility peaks.
</AnswerBox>

For a restaurant, a fake review costs a few $40 tickets. For a roofer, a fake review that pushes you under a prospect's mental cutoff can cost a $15,000 job — and during a busy storm season, several of them. The math isn't subtle. Homeowners hiring a roofer are scared of two things: getting ripped off and getting shoddy work that leaks two winters later. Reviews are how they manage that fear, which means a fake one-star isn't a minor annoyance. It's a direct hit to the trust that converts a lead into a signed contract.

<Definition term="Storm chaser">
An out-of-town or fly-by-night roofing operation that descends on an area after a major hail or wind event, often working from temporary contact info and disappearing once the storm work dries up. Storm chasers crowd the local market, sometimes leave fake reviews to inflate themselves or attack established local roofers, and frequently generate confused or angry homeowners who later post reviews aimed at the wrong company.
</Definition>

The storm cycle is what makes roofing reputation uniquely volatile. After a hailstorm, search volume for roofers spikes, your Google Business Profile gets more eyes than at any other time of year, and the local market fills with both desperate homeowners and opportunistic competitors. That's the worst possible moment to have a fake one-star sitting at the top of your profile — and it's precisely when they tend to appear.

## What kinds of fake and unfair reviews do roofers get?

<AnswerBox>
Roofers see a distinct set of fake and unfair reviews: out-of-town storm-chaser and competitor attacks posted after storms, "they never showed up" reviews from homeowners who actually contacted a different roofer, insurance-claim disputes spilling into reviews, and extortion attempts tied to deductibles or warranty claims. Each maps to a specific Google policy violation — and each requires roofing-specific evidence to prove.
</AnswerBox>

Here are the patterns that show up again and again on roofing profiles, and how each lines up with a removable violation.

- **Competitor attacks after a storm.** A rival roofer (or a storm chaser trying to thin the local field) posts a one-star posing as a customer. Tell-tale signs: the profile praises two or three of your local competitors and only trashes you, the review drops right after a storm or a bid you lost, and the language uses insider terms a real homeowner wouldn't ("they used cheap synthetic underlayment"). This is a **conflict-of-interest** violation when you can evidence the relationship.
- **"They never showed up / never called me back."** A homeowner contacts five roofers after a storm, gets overwhelmed, and leaves a one-star for the one they *think* ghosted them — except they actually called a different company with a similar name. You have no record of them because they were never your lead. This is **fake engagement** (a non-customer) and is highly removable with a clean no-match CRM search.
- **Insurance-dispute spillover.** A homeowner's claim gets denied or underpaid by their carrier, and they take the frustration out on you in a review, sometimes with false statements about work you performed. Honest frustration is protected; *false factual claims*, threats, or extortion are not.
- **Deductible extortion.** "Eat my deductible or I'm leaving this one-star up." That's a textbook **prohibited content / extortion** violation — among the strongest removal cases when you have it in writing.
- **Mistaken identity.** Common in roofing because business names cluster around city names and generic terms ("[City] Roofing," "Premier Roofing"). A review meant for a different company lands on yours — an **off-topic** violation.

<Callout type="warning">
Not every angry roofing review is removable. A homeowner who writes "the crew left roofing nails all over my driveway and lawn and my kid got a flat tire" is describing a real experience, even at one star. That's honest feedback Google protects — report it as fake and you waste your credibility. The right move is a calm, specific public reply. </Callout>

## What evidence gets a fake roofing review removed?

<AnswerBox>
Removals are won with roofing-specific documentation that proves either "this person was never our customer" or "this reviewer has a conflict of interest." The strongest roofing evidence is a no-match search of your CRM and job records, signed contracts and proposals, pulled permits tied to a property address, dated job-site and drone photos, and any written insurance or extortion correspondence. Lead with the single clearest fact — usually the empty customer-records search.
</AnswerBox>

Google's reviewers process huge volumes, so the easier you make the decision, the better your odds. Roofers have unusually strong documentation available — use it.

| Evidence type | Why it's powerful for roofers |
| --- | --- |
| **No-match CRM / job-record search** | A dated screenshot showing the reviewer's name, address, and phone produce zero matches is the headline for any "non-customer" case |
| **Signed contracts and proposals** | Proves exactly who you did and didn't do work for, with dates and property addresses |
| **Pulled permits** | Roofing permits are public, tied to addresses, and show whether you ever worked at the claimed property |
| **Dated job-site and drone photos** | Time-stamped photos prove the state and timeline of work, countering false factual claims |
| **Reviewer profile history** | A profile praising your competitors and only attacking you signals a competitor or coordinated attack |
| **Insurance / text correspondence** | Written extortion ("cover my deductible or this stays up") is among the strongest possible evidence |

The process for filing and escalating is the same one every contractor uses — choose the precise violation, attach your evidence, report from your verified owner account, and escalate if denied. The complete step-by-step lives in [how to remove a fake Google review](/blog/how-to-remove-fake-google-review). The roofing-specific part is *which records you bring*, and roofers who keep tight contracts, permits, and photo logs have a real advantage here.

<Callout type="tip">
Build the evidence habit before you need it. Roofers who log every job with a signed contract, the pulled permit number, and dated drone photos can assemble a removal case in minutes instead of scrambling after a fake appears mid-storm-season. Your job records are also your reputation defense.
</Callout>

## How does this fit with the rest of your roofing reputation?

<AnswerBox>
Removal is one piece of a four-part system every roofer should run: earn reviews at the moment each roof is completed, respond professionally to honest negatives, defend against genuine policy-violating fakes, and keep your profile active so you rank in the local pack — especially before storm season. Because roofing is a once-a-decade purchase, capturing each review at completion is critical, and a deep, recent profile is your best insurance against the next fake.
</AnswerBox>

Roofing sits at the high-ticket, one-time, storm-driven corner of the trade map — which means each review carries outsized weight and fakes are harder to dilute than in high-frequency trades. That makes two habits non-negotiable. First, capture a review the day you finish each roof; you may not have another chance with that customer for ten years. Second, keep building volume in calm months so that when a storm-season fake lands, it's one one-star among many recent fives instead of a glaring mark on a thin profile.
