Some bad reviews aren't from customers at all — they're from the shop across town that wants your phone to stop ringing. A competitor posing as a homeowner, a fired installer with an axe to grind, or someone with a personal grudge can plant a one-star review that reads plausibly and quietly bleeds you of jobs. These reviews violate Google's conflict-of-interest policy and are removable. This guide shows contractors how to spot a competitor review, build the conflict-of-interest case, and gather the evidence that actually gets it taken down.

## Can you remove a fake review left by a competitor?

<AnswerBox>
Yes. A review from a competitor violates Google's conflict-of-interest policy, which prohibits reviews from anyone with a stake in your business's reputation rather than a genuine customer experience. Competitors, current and former employees, and people with a personal grudge all qualify. The work is in proving the connection — the stronger and more documented the relationship, the more likely Google removes the review.
</AnswerBox>

The conflict-of-interest policy exists precisely because reviews are supposed to reflect real experiences from real customers. When the person writing the review stands to gain from harming you — a rival who wants your customers, an ex-employee who wants revenge — the review isn't feedback. It's sabotage wearing a customer's clothes.

<Definition term="Conflict of interest review">
A Google review posted by someone who has a competing or personal stake in your business's reputation instead of an honest customer experience — for example a competitor posing as a customer, a current or former employee, or someone with a personal grudge. Because the reviewer's motive is to harm (or benefit) the business rather than describe a service, the review violates Google's conflict-of-interest policy and is eligible for removal.
</Definition>

The honest caveat we put on every page: this only works because the review genuinely violates policy. A real customer who happens to also dislike you, or who switched to a competitor and left an honest complaint, is still a real customer — their review isn't a conflict of interest and can't be removed. The conflict-of-interest case is about the *reviewer's relationship to your business*, not about how negative the words are. If you're not sure which category your review falls into, start with [how to tell if a Google review violates policy](/blog/how-to-tell-if-google-review-violates-policy).

## How do you spot a competitor's fake review?

<AnswerBox>
Spot a competitor review by looking for what a real customer would have and a saboteur wouldn't: no specific service details, a profile tied to a rival business, suspicious timing around a lost bid or firing, "industry-aware" complaints phrased like an insider, and a pattern of rating you down while rating your competitor up. Any one of these is a clue; several together build a case.
</AnswerBox>

Competitor reviews are designed to look real, so you diagnose them by the absence of the things genuine reviews carry and the presence of things only an insider would do.

**No real service details.** A genuine homeowner mentions the date, the crew, the specific job — "they replaced my AC unit in July and left the old one in the yard." A competitor's review stays vague because they weren't there: "terrible work, total ripoff, avoid."

**Insider language.** A competitor knows the trade and accidentally reveals it. Complaints about your "markup on materials," "permit handling," or "subcontractor crews" phrased like someone in the business — not like a worried homeowner — are a tell.

**Suspicious timing.** Competitor and ex-employee reviews cluster around business events, not service events. A one-star landing days after you won a contract a rival wanted, or a week after you fired someone, is rarely coincidence.

**The you-down, rival-up pattern.** Click the reviewer's profile. If they left you one star and your nearest competitor five stars — especially with more detail in the competitor's glowing review — that's a pattern, not a customer.

**Profile ties to a competing business.** Sometimes it's right there: the reviewer's profile name, photo, or other reviews connect them to a rival contractor.

## Competitor review red flags: a reference table

Use this to triage a suspicious review. The more rows you can check, the stronger your conflict-of-interest case.

| Red flag | Why it points to a competitor | What to capture |
| --- | --- | --- |
| No date, crew name, or job specifics | Real customers reference their actual experience | Screenshot of the vague review |
| "Industry-aware" complaints (markup, permits, subs) | Phrasing reveals trade knowledge a homeowner wouldn't use | Highlight the insider language |
| Posted right after a lost bid, win, or firing | Tied to a business event, not a service | Event-to-review timeline with dates |
| You one star, local competitor five stars | A coordinated rating pattern, not feedback | Side-by-side profile screenshots |
| Reviewer profile links to a rival business | Direct evidence of the competing stake | Screenshot of the connecting profile |
| Reviewer is a former employee | Ex-employees have a grudge, not a customer relationship | Employment + termination records |
| No match in your customer records | If they never hired you, they're not a customer | Dated "no record found" screenshot |

<Callout type="warning">
One red flag alone usually isn't enough — and don't talk yourself into seeing a competitor where there's just an unhappy customer. A real client who later became a fan of your rival can still leave an honest one-star review about you. The conflict-of-interest case needs the relationship to be the point. Stack several red flags before you commit.
</Callout>

## How do you build the conflict-of-interest case?

<AnswerBox>
Build the case by stacking circumstantial evidence into a single clear story: this reviewer was never your customer, here's their tie to a competitor (or their ex-employment), here's the timing that lines up with a business event, and here's the rating pattern. No one item proves it alone — the combination does. Lead with your single strongest fact and support it with the rest.
</AnswerBox>

Conflict-of-interest cases are rarely decided by one smoking gun. They're decided by a stack of facts that, together, make "honest customer" impossible to believe. Here's how to assemble it.

**Step 1: Confirm they were never a customer.** Search your CRM, invoicing, and job calendar for the reviewer's name, phone, address, and email. A clean miss is your foundation — it removes the "maybe they really were a client" defense.

**Step 2: Establish the competing stake.** Screenshot whatever ties them to a rival: a profile that names or promotes a competing business, other reviews that boost your competitor, or — for an ex-employee — employment and termination records.

**Step 3: Build the timeline.** Write a short, dated sequence: the triggering event (lost bid, contract win, firing, refusal) and when the review appeared. Proximity is persuasive.

**Step 4: Document the pattern.** Capture the you-down/rival-up behavior and any mass-review activity across local businesses.

**Step 5: Save any communication.** Texts, emails, or DMs that reveal who they are — a competitor slipping up, an ex-employee threatening "you'll regret this" — are the strongest single pieces when you have them.

<Callout type="tip">
Write your case as one tight paragraph before you report. Lead with the strongest fact ("This reviewer is the owner of [rival company]" or "This person is not in our customer records"), then list the supporting evidence. Google's reviewers move fast — make your strongest point first and make it impossible to miss.
</Callout>

## What if you can't identify the competitor?

<AnswerBox>
If you can't prove who the competitor is, pivot to a fake-engagement report instead. The fact that the reviewer was never your customer — no job, no invoice, no record — is its own strong, removable violation. Conflict of interest and fake engagement overlap heavily, so lead with whichever you can actually evidence, and let "never a customer" carry the case when identity is murky.
</AnswerBox>

You don't need to unmask the person to win. In practice, many competitor reviews are reported successfully as fake engagement, because the cleanest fact available is that the reviewer simply was never a customer. That's a documentable absence — a dated screenshot of an empty search in your system — and it doesn't require you to prove anyone's identity.

So when the conflict-of-interest evidence is thin:

- Lead with the customer-record miss. "No record of this person in our system" is concrete and verifiable.
- Add whatever circumstantial competitor evidence you do have as support, even if it's not conclusive.
- Pick the violation category you can best prove when you file. The [pillar guide on removing fake Google reviews](/blog/how-to-remove-fake-google-review) covers exactly how to choose the right category and submit the report.

## How do you report a competitor review to Google?

<AnswerBox>
Report it from your Google Business Profile: open the review, choose "Report review," and select conflict of interest (or fake engagement if identity is unclear). State the case plainly in the text field — who the reviewer is, that they were never a customer, and the relationship — then track the report and escalate with your evidence if it stalls.
</AnswerBox>

The reporting mechanics are the same as any removal, but the framing is what wins a conflict-of-interest case. Here's the sequence:

1. **Report from the owner account.** File from your verified Google Business Profile, not a personal account — owner reports carry more weight.
2. **Pick the right category.** Choose conflict of interest when you can tie the reviewer to a competitor or ex-employment. If you can only prove "never a customer," choose fake engagement.
3. **State the case in facts.** "This reviewer is the owner of [rival business] and was never a paying customer of ours." No ranting — dates, names, relationship.
4. **Track and escalate.** If there's no decision in about two weeks, escalate through Google Business Profile support and attach your evidence file: the connecting profile, the timeline, the pattern, the record miss.
5. **Expect to persist.** Conflict-of-interest cases are often denied on the automated first pass because the connection isn't obvious to a machine. A clearer resubmission with the relationship spelled out is frequently what gets it removed.
