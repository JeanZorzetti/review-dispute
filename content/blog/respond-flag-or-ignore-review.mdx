Every review that lands on your profile is really a decision, not just a notification. You've got four moves available: reply to it publicly, flag it to Google for a policy violation, do both, or leave it alone. Most contractors default to one habit — some reply to everything, some report everything they don't like, some ignore it all — and all three defaults are wrong some of the time. The skill is matching the move to the review. This is the decision framework: a clear way to look at any review and know, in under a minute, whether to respond, flag, do both, or step back.

## Should you respond to or report a review?

<AnswerBox>
It depends entirely on the type of review, because responding and reporting do two different jobs. A reply is public and speaks to every future customer who reads your profile; it never removes anything. A report (flag) is private and asks Google to take the review down — but only works when the review actually violates a policy. So a legitimate negative gets a reply, a fake or rule-breaking review gets a reply plus a report, a positive review gets a short thank-you, and obvious spam or abuse gets reported. Often the right answer is "both," not "one or the other."
</AnswerBox>

The single biggest mistake contractors make is treating "respond" and "report" as the same decision — as if you pick one. They're not even the same kind of action. One is a public statement aimed at your future customers. The other is a private request aimed at Google's review team. They don't compete; for a lot of reviews you'll want to do both, and for plenty you'll do just one.

## What's the difference between responding and reporting?

<AnswerBox>
Responding means posting a public reply that appears beneath the review — it persuades future readers but leaves the review in place permanently. Reporting (also called flagging) means privately notifying Google that the review violates its content policies and asking for removal — it's the only path to taking a review down, and it only succeeds when the review actually breaks a rule. Responding is for the audience; reporting is for the platform. A great reply and a successful report achieve completely different outcomes.
</AnswerBox>

<Definition term="Responding (replying)">
Posting a public, visible reply beneath a review through your Google Business Profile. It addresses future readers and the reviewer, demonstrates professionalism, and stays up permanently — but it does not and cannot remove the review.
</Definition>

<Definition term="Reporting (flagging)">
Privately notifying Google that a review violates its content policies and requesting its removal. It's invisible to the public, it's the only route to getting a review taken down, and it only works for reviews that genuinely break a policy — not for honest negatives you simply dislike.
</Definition>

The practical consequence is what trips people up. If a competitor leaves you a fake one-star and you write a brilliant, calm public reply — that review is still there. Your reply persuades readers, which is valuable, but it doesn't remove anything. To get it down, you have to *also* report it, and it has to actually violate a policy. Conversely, reporting an honest unhappy customer's review is a dead end: it doesn't break any rule, so Google leaves it up, and you've wasted the one move (a good reply) that would have actually helped. Knowing which tool does which job is most of the battle.

## The decision framework: respond, flag, both, or leave it

<AnswerBox>
Run every review through one question first — is this a genuine customer experience or not? If it's genuine, you respond (briefly for positives, with the full framework for negatives) and you do not report, because honest reviews don't violate policy. If it's not genuine — a non-customer, a competitor, spam, abuse, or extortion — you respond with a short factual note for readers and report it to Google. The only reviews you might simply leave are obvious spam where a report is filed and no public reply adds anything.
</AnswerBox>

Here's the whole framework in one table. Find the review type, and you have your move.

| Review type | Respond? | Report? | Why |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Legitimate negative (honest unhappy customer) | Yes — full framework | No | Honest reviews don't violate policy; a calm reply wins future customers |
| Fake review (non-customer / competitor) | Yes — short, factual | Yes | Reply for readers; report because it violates policy. See [respond to a fake review](/blog/how-to-respond-to-fake-review) |
| Review you're actively disputing | Yes — neutral, careful | Yes | Reply without undermining the case; see [respond while disputing](/blog/respond-to-review-youre-disputing) |
| Positive review | Yes — brief thank-you | No | Cheap, high-trust signal; never leave a good review hanging |
| Spam (gibberish, ads, bots) | Optional / usually no | Yes | No real customer to address; flag for removal |
| Abusive content (slurs, threats) | Optional / brief | Yes | Clear policy violation; report it; reply only if a calm note helps |
| Extortion ("pay or it stays") | Yes — calm, documented | Yes | Reply neutrally; report; see [handle review extortion](/blog/handle-review-extortion-professionally) |
| Off-topic (about another business, wrong company) | Optional | Yes | Violates relevance policy; flag it |

Notice the pattern. The line that decides everything is **genuine vs. not genuine.** Genuine reviews — positive or negative — get a response and never a report, because they don't break any rule. Non-genuine reviews get reported, and usually a short reply too, so the audience sees your side while the report works its way through Google. If you're unsure whether something actually violates a policy, the cleanest reference is [Google's review policy explained](/blog/google-review-policy-explained) — that's what determines whether a report has any chance.

<Callout type="tip">
When in doubt between "respond" and "respond + report," lean toward doing both — as long as the review plausibly violates a policy. A short, neutral public reply never hurts you, and a report runs separately in the background. The only thing to watch: keep the public reply calm and factual so it doesn't accidentally weaken your removal case. Combative replies can make a reported review look like a normal business dispute rather than a policy violation.
</Callout>

## Why "ignore" is almost never the right answer

<AnswerBox>
Ignoring a review is rarely the best move because silence reads to future customers as not caring or not paying attention. For negatives, an unanswered one-star looks like you had nothing to say for yourself. For positives, an unanswered five-star wastes a free chance to look engaged and gracious. The only defensible version of "ignore" is filing a report on obvious spam or bot content and not writing a public reply — because there's no real customer to address and a reply adds nothing.
</AnswerBox>

"I'll just ignore it and it'll sink down the page" is the most common reputation mistake in the trades, and it almost always costs you. Future customers don't just read reviews; they read *which reviews you bothered to respond to.* A negative with no reply tells them you either didn't notice or didn't care enough to address it — both are bad. A positive with no reply tells them you take good customers for granted. Doing nothing isn't neutral; it's a message, and it's the wrong one.

There's exactly one situation where something close to ignoring is fine: pure spam or bot content — gibberish, an ad for crypto, a review clearly meant for a different business. There, you report it for removal and skip the public reply, because there's no real person and no real experience to speak to. Even then, you've technically taken an action (the report). True do-nothing-at-all should be reserved for the rarest cases, not used as a default.

## Putting the framework to work

<AnswerBox>
Build the framework into a quick routine: when a review lands, first ask whether it's a genuine customer experience. If yes, respond — briefly for positives, with the full framework for negatives — and don't report. If no, respond with a short factual note and report it for the policy violation. Reserve "leave it entirely" for obvious spam you've already flagged. Run this check on every review and you'll never again default to over-replying, over-reporting, or ignoring.
</AnswerBox>

The whole point of a framework is that you stop deciding from emotion and start deciding from type. An angry one-star from a real customer feels like it deserves a report — but it doesn't qualify for one, so you reply instead and win the readers. A polite-sounding review from someone who was never your customer feels like it deserves a measured reply and nothing more — but it's a genuine policy violation, so you reply *and* report. The framework keeps you from letting the tone of a review fool you about the right move.
