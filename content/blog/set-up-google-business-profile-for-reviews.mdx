Most contractors have a Google Business Profile. Most of those profiles are incomplete, unverified, or missing the one setting that makes reviews effortless to collect. The profile itself isn't the hard part of getting reviews — the ask is. But a poorly set-up profile adds friction to every request you make, and friction is the enemy of follow-through. This guide is the practical setup playbook: get your profile in the state where asking a customer for a review takes one tap on their phone, not five steps and a Google search.

## Why does your Google Business Profile setup directly affect how many reviews you get?

<AnswerBox>
A well-set-up profile makes reviewing frictionless: your direct review link opens the form in one tap, your profile looks complete and trustworthy so customers feel comfortable taking two minutes to write something, and Google ranks complete profiles higher so more people find you in the first place. An incomplete or unverified profile creates friction at every stage — customers who try to find you to leave a review sometimes can't, and the profile that does show up may have wrong information that damages the credibility you need them to trust.
</AnswerBox>

Think of your Google Business Profile as the foundation for every review you'll ever collect. The ask itself — how you approach a customer at job close, the timing, the words you use — matters a lot. But even a perfect ask fails if the customer has to search for your business, click through three screens, and figure out where the "Write a review" button is. That's not an unusual customer. That's most customers. Most people who would leave a review don't because it's just inconvenient enough that they don't bother.

The setup work in this guide eliminates that friction. By the end of it, you'll have a link you can text to a customer that opens their review form in one tap — no searching required — and a profile that looks like a real, established business, because trust is part of what motivates a customer to spend two minutes writing about you.

<Definition term="Google Business Profile (GBP)">
The free business listing that appears in Google Search and Google Maps, showing your business name, phone number, address (or service area), hours, photos, and customer reviews. For home-service contractors, it's typically the first thing a homeowner sees when searching for a roofer, HVAC company, or plumber in their area, and it's the platform where Google Reviews are collected and displayed.
</Definition>

## Step 1: Claim and verify your profile — this is non-negotiable

<AnswerBox>
If your profile isn't verified, customers cannot leave reviews, period. Verification is the first step, not an afterthought. Go to business.google.com, search for your business, and either claim an existing listing or create a new one. Google will prompt you through the verification process — video verification is the fastest option for most contractors and can be approved within hours to a couple of days.
</AnswerBox>

Go to **business.google.com** and sign in with the Google account you want to manage your business profile. Search for your business name. One of two things will happen:

1. **A listing already exists.** This happens frequently for contractors — a listing was auto-generated from data Google pulled from other sources, or a previous employee or owner created one. Claim it. There will be an "Own this business?" option. Don't create a duplicate; claim the existing one.
2. **No listing exists.** Create one. You'll enter your business name, primary category, and address or service area.

Once you've claimed or created the listing, Google requires verification before the profile goes live and accepts reviews. Your verification options:

- **Video verification** — Record a short video of your business location, your signage, equipment, or vehicles with your company name visible. This is the fastest method and is the primary option Google presents to most new or recently claimed profiles. Approval typically comes within hours to 48 hours.
- **Postcard by mail** — Google mails a postcard with a verification code to your business address. This takes 5-14 days. If you work from home or a service area without a physical office, make sure the address you use can receive mail.
- **Phone or email verification** — Some accounts are eligible for this, which is essentially instant. If Google offers it, take it.

<Callout type="warning">
Do not skip verification and start asking customers for reviews. If your profile is unverified, reviews customers try to leave will either fail silently or get held in a queue that never publishes. You'll be collecting goodwill and wasting it. Verify first. Everything else comes after.
</Callout>

## Step 2: Choose the right primary category for your trade

<AnswerBox>
Your primary category is one of the strongest ranking signals in your profile. Choose the most specific accurate match for your primary trade: "Roofing Contractor" for roofers, "HVAC Contractor" for heating and cooling companies, "Plumber" or "Plumbing Service" for plumbers. Contractors who choose a generic category like "General Contractor" when they primarily do roofing will rank lower for roofing searches than a competitor who has it set correctly.
</AnswerBox>

When you're in your profile dashboard, go to **Business information > Category**. The primary category is the main one — there's one primary and you can add secondary categories.

Category selection by trade:

- **Roofing:** Primary — "Roofing Contractor." Secondary categories can include "Gutter Installation Service," "Siding Contractor," or "Skylight Contractor" if you offer those services.
- **HVAC:** Primary — "HVAC Contractor." Secondaries for "Air Conditioning Repair Service," "Furnace Repair Service," or "Heat Pump Supplier" depending on what you do.
- **Plumbing:** Primary — "Plumber" or "Plumbing Service." Secondaries for "Drainage Service," "Water Heater Installation Service," or "Water Treatment Supplier" as appropriate.
- **General electrical:** "Electrician." Secondary "Electrical Installation Service."

Secondary categories help you show up for related searches, but the primary category is where the ranking weight lives. Set it correctly before worrying about anything else, because it's the foundation of your local search visibility and changing it after you've established a profile ranking has a cost.

<Callout type="tip">
If you're a multi-trade contractor (roofing and siding, or HVAC and plumbing), choose the trade that generates the most revenue as your primary category. You can set up a second profile for a second location if you operate across genuinely separate service areas, but don't create duplicate profiles for the same location — Google penalizes that.
</Callout>

## Step 3: Complete every section of your profile

<AnswerBox>
Profile completeness affects both ranking and conversion. Google rewards complete profiles with better visibility, and homeowners trust profiles that look fully filled out. The sections that matter most for contractor profiles are your business description, service area, services list, hours, and phone number. Incomplete profiles also let Google auto-populate information from third-party sources, which is frequently wrong and can confuse or misdirect customers.
</AnswerBox>

Work through each section methodically:

**Business name.** This should be your legal business name or the name you consistently use everywhere — no keyword-stuffing ("Bob's Roofing Best Roofer Columbus Ohio"). Google has taken action against keyword-stuffed names, and it looks unprofessional to homeowners.

**Address or service area.** If you have a physical office that customers visit, enter it. If you're a service-area business that works from home or a warehouse not open to the public, hide the address and set your service area by city or county instead. For most residential contractors, service-area mode is the right choice — you don't want the map pin pointing to your house.

**Phone number.** Use your primary business line. This is what Google uses to match your profile to other mentions of your business around the web, so consistency matters — the same number should appear on your website, your invoices, and your directories.

**Business hours.** Fill these in accurately. Mark holiday hours when relevant. An incorrect "open now" status that sends a homeowner to your voicemail mid-afternoon damages trust, and Google's algorithm does pick up on user interactions that suggest your listed hours are wrong.

**Business description.** You have 750 characters. Use the first two sentences to describe what you do and where — "Valley Roofing has replaced and repaired roofs for homeowners across the Columbus metro since 2008. We specialize in asphalt shingle replacement, storm damage repair, and full tear-off re-roofing." Don't stuff keywords; write for the homeowner who'll read it.

**Services.** Add your specific services — "Asphalt shingle installation," "Emergency roof repair," "HVAC tune-up," "Water heater replacement." Be specific. Services show up in searches and help Google understand what you do.

**Attributes.** These are the checkboxes — "Veteran-owned," "Licensed," "Insured," "Free estimates." Check every attribute that accurately applies to your business.

## Step 4: Add contractor-specific photos that build trust

<AnswerBox>
Photos are one of the highest-trust signals in a contractor's profile — homeowners are letting a stranger on their property, and photos of a real crew, real job sites, and real completed work do more to build that trust than any amount of text. Add a minimum of 10-15 photos: before-and-after job shots, your crew in branded uniforms at an active job, your branded vehicles, and any equipment or product displays. Update photos monthly — fresh imagery signals an active business.
</AnswerBox>

Photo quality matters here, but authenticity matters more than production value. A crisp photo from an iPhone of your crew on a rooftop, or a before/after of an HVAC installation, outperforms a stock photo of a smiling homeowner shaking hands with an actor in a hard hat.

**The photos that convert:**

- **Before/after job shots.** For roofers: the old damaged shingles versus the finished replacement. For HVAC: the old unit sitting outside versus the new installation. For plumbers: corroded pipes versus new copper. These show competence in a way that words can't.
- **Crew on a job site.** Real people in branded shirts or uniforms at a real job. This signals that you have an established operation, not a one-man show operating from a pickup truck.
- **Branded vehicles.** Your fleet with the logo visible. Homeowners who've seen your truck in the neighborhood often search to find you — your photos help them confirm they've found the right company.
- **Certifications and product displays.** If you're a certified installer for a specific shingle brand, or an HVAC manufacturer's preferred dealer, photos of those credentials or branded product displays add credibility.

**What to avoid:**

- Stock photos. Google's systems can often detect them, and homeowners definitely can. A profile full of stock photos reads as a business with nothing real to show.
- Low-quality blurry images. You don't need a professional photographer, but photos should be in focus, reasonably lit, and shot horizontally.
- Photos that include visible personal information (house numbers, homeowners' faces without permission).

Set a recurring reminder to add 2-4 new photos monthly. Google favors profiles with regular activity, and photos are one of the easiest ways to signal that you're active.

## Step 5: Get your direct review link and QR code

<AnswerBox>
Your direct review link is the asset that makes reviews one tap away. Go to business.google.com, open your profile, and click "Ask for reviews." Google displays a short link that takes customers directly to your review form. Copy it, shorten it if needed, and use it everywhere: texts, emails, invoices, leave-behind cards. Generate a QR code from it for print materials. This is the single highest-ROI setup task in your entire profile.
</AnswerBox>

Here's exactly how to get it:

1. Go to **business.google.com** and log in.
2. Select your profile from the list (if you have multiple locations).
3. In the left navigation or on the main dashboard, find the **"Get more reviews"** card, or go to **Home > Ask for reviews**.
4. Google will display a short shareable link — something like `g.page/YourBusinessName/review` or a `g.co/kgs/` style URL.
5. Copy that link.
6. Shorten it if it's long — use **bit.ly** or a similar tool to create a clean, short URL you can remember and text quickly.
7. Test it. Open an incognito browser window, paste the link, and confirm it takes you straight to the "Write a review" modal for your business.

**Creating a QR code.** Once you have your shortened review link, use any free QR code generator (QR Code Generator, qr-code-monkey.com, or the one built into Canva) to create a QR code. This QR code goes on:

- Your leave-behind card you hand to every customer after a completed job
- The footer of your invoice
- A laminated card you keep in your truck
- A sign in your showroom or office if customers come to you

The QR code is specifically useful for in-person moments — you're doing the final walkthrough with a homeowner, they're happy, you hand them a card. "If you're happy with the job, I'd really appreciate a quick Google review — just scan this." That card does the asking even when you don't have the words.

The full ask sequence — what to say, when to say it, how to follow up — is in [review request templates](/blog/review-request-templates) and the timing science is in [best time to ask for a review](/blog/best-time-to-ask-for-a-review).

<Callout type="tip">
Test your review link from a phone every time you update your profile. Google occasionally changes short link formats after profile edits, and a broken review link is invisible to you but a dead end for every customer you send there. Check it when you set it up, and recheck it once a month.
</Callout>

## Step 6: Set up review notifications so you can respond fast

<AnswerBox>
Responding to reviews within 24-48 hours signals active management to Google and builds trust with every future homeowner who reads your profile. Set up email notifications for new reviews in your Google Business Profile dashboard so you never miss one. A response routine — thank the positive ones by name and job reference, acknowledge and de-escalate the negative ones — is part of what makes a profile look lived-in and trustworthy rather than neglected.
</AnswerBox>

Notifications are configured in your Google Business Profile settings:

1. Open **business.google.com**.
2. Click your profile, then go to **Settings > Notifications**.
3. Enable "New reviews" notifications to your email address.
4. If you manage the profile from the Google Maps app on your phone, enable push notifications there as well.

Once notifications are set, build a simple response habit. When a new review comes in:

- **For a positive review:** Respond within 24 hours. Use the customer's first name if it's in the review, reference the job type, and thank them genuinely. "Thanks, Jennifer — glad the new shingles look exactly how you wanted. Really appreciate you taking the time to write this." That's enough. Don't use a template that clearly gets copy-pasted on every review.
- **For a negative review:** Respond calmly, acknowledge their concern publicly, and offer to resolve it offline. The homeowners reading your profile aren't evaluating whether you ever get a complaint — they're evaluating how you handle it when you do.

Responding consistently also matters to Google. An active, engaged profile is treated as a more trustworthy signal than one where reviews pile up unanswered. The response strategy in detail is in [how to respond to negative Google reviews](/blog/how-to-respond-to-negative-google-reviews) and covers the tonal balance of handling criticism professionally without being defensive.

## Step 7: Connect your profile to your broader review system

<AnswerBox>
Your Google Business Profile setup is the foundation, not the whole system. The review link and QR code you've set up need to be woven into every customer interaction — the job-close moment, the invoice, the follow-up text. Profile completeness gets you ranked and ready; the actual review volume comes from asking consistently on every completed job. The profile and the ask process together are what make reviews predictable instead of random.
</AnswerBox>

Once the profile is fully set up, the work shifts from configuration to habit. The profile makes reviewing easy; the ask makes it happen. Here's how they connect:

**At job close.** After the final walkthrough, when the homeowner has said something positive: "Really glad it turned out well. If you have two minutes, an honest Google review would mean a lot to us — I can text you the link right now, it takes one tap." Send the link on the spot. That moment — warm customer, job just completed, phone in hand — is the highest-conversion ask you'll ever make. The timing science behind this is in [best time to ask for a review](/blog/best-time-to-ask-for-a-review).

**On the invoice.** Add your review link (or QR code) to the invoice footer. Something like "Satisfied with our work? Leave us a quick Google review:" followed by the link. This reaches customers who didn't respond to the in-person ask and look at their paperwork later.

**In the follow-up text.** For customers who received the in-person ask but didn't follow through, a single polite follow-up 3-5 days after job close converts a meaningful percentage. Keep it short: "Hi [Name] — just checking that everything with the [job type] is still looking good. If you have a minute, a Google review would really help our small business: [link]"

What you never do is filter who gets asked — happy customers only, or customers you're confident will say something positive. Asking everyone the same way is the policy-compliant standard, and it's covered fully in [is it against Google's rules to ask for reviews](/blog/is-it-against-google-rules-to-ask-for-reviews). The full end-to-end ask process, with templates for text and email, is in [review request templates](/blog/review-request-templates).

<Callout type="info">
Your Google Business Profile is a live document, not a one-time setup. Revisit it quarterly: update photos, add new services you've started offering, check that your hours are correct, and confirm your review link still works. Profiles that show regular activity rank better and convert better than ones that were set up once and forgotten.
</Callout>

## What about profiles with an existing problem review situation?

If you're setting up or cleaning up your profile and there are reviews that appear to be fake, from competitors, or from people who were never your customers, those are a separate issue from the optimization work above. Google's policy allows removal of reviews that violate its content guidelines — reviews from non-customers, spam, off-topic content, prohibited material. The profile setup and the review removal process are independent, and doing the setup work doesn't affect your ability to dispute problematic reviews.

## Your profile setup checklist

Before you start asking customers for reviews, confirm you've done all of this:

- [ ] Profile claimed and fully verified
- [ ] Primary category correctly set to your trade
- [ ] Service area or physical address correct
- [ ] Business hours accurate including holidays
- [ ] Business description written (750 chars, first two sentences specific)
- [ ] Services list complete
- [ ] All applicable attributes checked (Licensed, Insured, etc.)
- [ ] Minimum 10 photos uploaded — job sites, crew, before/after, vehicles
- [ ] Direct review link obtained, shortened, and tested
- [ ] QR code generated and added to invoice and leave-behind cards
- [ ] Review notifications enabled by email and/or mobile push
- [ ] Review response habit established (within 24-48 hours)

That's the complete setup. A contractor with this foundation in place turns every satisfied customer into a potential review with one tap and no friction. The reviews that follow are the engine behind [growing review volume consistently](/blog/how-to-get-more-google-reviews-contractors) — and consistent, genuine review volume is the reputation that wins jobs for years.
