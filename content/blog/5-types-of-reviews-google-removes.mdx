## What types of reviews does Google remove?

<AnswerBox>
Google removes five types of reviews: fake engagement (reviews from people who never used your service), conflict of interest (reviews from competitors, employees, or the owner), off-topic (content not about an actual experience with your business), spam (ads, links, gibberish, and duplicates), and prohibited or restricted content (threats, hate speech, harassment, and personal information). A review must fit one of these five categories — and you must be able to evidence it — to be eligible for removal.
</AnswerBox>

These five categories come straight out of Google's contributed-content policy, the rulebook that governs everything people post about your business. They're not suggestions or vibes; they're the entire universe of removable content. If a review fits one and you can prove it, it's a candidate for removal. If it fits none, it stays — full stop. For the broader picture of how this policy works end to end, the [complete Google review policy guide](/blog/google-review-policy-explained) is the hub; this article zooms into the five removable types one by one.

<Definition term="Removable category">
One of the five families of content that Google's policy prohibits in reviews. A review qualifies for removal only if it fits at least one removable category and the violation can be demonstrated. A review that's merely negative, mistaken, or unflattering — but posted honestly by a real customer — fits none of the categories and is not removable.
</Definition>

## Type 1: Fake engagement (the reviewer was never your customer)

<AnswerBox>
Fake engagement is a review from someone who never actually used your service — a fabricated review, a retaliation post from a non-customer, or a rating designed to manipulate your score rather than reflect a real experience. The defining test is whether the person appears in your customer records. If there's no job, no invoice, and no match in your system, you likely have a fake-engagement violation.
</AnswerBox>

This is the most common removable type for home-services businesses and the easiest to prove, because it turns on a fact you fully control: your own records.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- A one-star from a name you've never heard, with no address, job date, or service details.
- A review describing work you don't even offer ("they wrecked my pool install" when you're strictly HVAC).
- A burst of low ratings in a single day, often with no text.
- A reviewer profile that has rated dozens of unrelated businesses across different cities in a short window.

**Tell-tale signs:** no details a real customer would know (the crew's name, the date, the specific job); a clean miss when you search the name, phone, and email in your CRM and invoicing; generic filler like "terrible service, would not recommend" with nothing concrete.

**How to report it:** flag the review, choose the fake/no-experience option, and lead with one sentence — *this person is not in our customer records.* Attach a dated screenshot of the empty search and the reviewer's scattershot profile. Fake engagement overlaps heavily with spam in Google's tooling, so the in-depth playbook for both lives in [spam and fake engagement reviews](/blog/spam-fake-engagement-reviews).

<Callout type="warning">
A bare one-star with no comment is NOT automatically fake. Real customers leave star-only ratings constantly. The missing detail is a reason to investigate, not proof. You only have a case once your records confirm the person was never a customer.
</Callout>

## Type 2: Conflict of interest (competitor, employee, or owner)

<AnswerBox>
A conflict-of-interest review comes from someone with a stake in your reputation rather than a real customer experience — most often a competitor posing as a customer, a current or former employee, or the business owner self-reviewing. The review is a weapon aimed at your rating, not feedback about a service the person paid for.
</AnswerBox>

This type does outsized damage because the people behind it know exactly where to hit. A rival contractor knows which words scare homeowners; a fired installer knows your weak spots. The review reads plausibly even though it's poisoned at the source.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- A "review" from the owner or an employee of a competing roofing, HVAC, or plumbing company.
- A former employee trashing you days after termination.
- A review timed suspiciously — right after you declined a job, lost a bid, or let someone go.
- A pattern where one profile gives you one star and your local competitor five.

**Tell-tale signs:** no service details, but unusually "industry-aware" complaints a normal homeowner wouldn't phrase that way; a reviewer profile that links to or promotes a rival; timing that lines up with a business event, not a service event.

**How to report it:** flag the review, choose conflict of interest, and attach whatever establishes the relationship — the reviewer's profile showing they work for a competitor, employment and termination records for an ex-employee, or screenshots of the suspicious pattern. The full evidence playbook is in [conflict-of-interest reviews](/blog/conflict-of-interest-reviews).

## Type 3: Off-topic (it's not about your service)

<AnswerBox>
An off-topic review doesn't describe an experience with your business at all. It might be a case of mistaken identity (a review meant for a different company), a general rant about contractors or an industry, or personal and political commentary unrelated to the service you provided. Off-topic content is eligible for removal because reviews are supposed to be about a real transaction with the business.
</AnswerBox>

Contractors underuse this type because it feels less dramatic than a fake — but it's often the cleanest to prove, because the review itself contains the evidence.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- A review clearly meant for another business with a similar name in another town.
- A rant about "all roofers being crooks" with nothing specific to you.
- Commentary about your politics, your truck's bumper sticker, or a news story — not your work.
- A complaint about a third party (a supplier, manufacturer, or the permit office) pinned on you.

**Tell-tale signs:** the review names a different company, location, or service you don't provide; the content is a general opinion, not an account of a transaction; the subject is outside the service relationship entirely.

**How to report it:** flag the review, choose off-topic, and screenshot the specific text that proves it — the wrong business name, the general-rant language, the unrelated subject. For mistaken identity, note the other business it actually fits. The detailed approach, including how to frame an off-topic argument, is in [off-topic reviews and Google policy](/blog/off-topic-reviews-google-policy).

## Type 4: Spam (ads, links, and duplicates)

<AnswerBox>
A spam review isn't genuine feedback — it's promotional content, a link drop, gibberish, or the same review duplicated across listings. The reviewer is using your profile to advertise, spread links, or game the system rather than describe your service. Spam is almost always removable because it has nothing to do with an actual experience.
</AnswerBox>

Spam shows up on contractor profiles more than you'd expect, and it's usually a clean case.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- A "review" that's really an ad for another product or service, often with a URL or phone number.
- The exact same text posted on your profile and several other local businesses.
- Bot-like gibberish, random characters, or keyword-stuffed nonsense.
- Posts soliciting you or your customers to a third-party site.

**Tell-tale signs:** links, promo codes, or contact info for an unrelated business; identical or near-identical text on multiple listings; content that reads like marketing or automated output, not a human customer.

**How to report it:** flag the review, choose spam, and screenshot the promotional content or link — and for duplicates, capture the same review on other businesses' profiles as supporting proof. Spam and fake engagement are handled together in [spam and fake engagement reviews](/blog/spam-fake-engagement-reviews).

## Type 5: Prohibited and restricted content (threats, hate, personal info)

<AnswerBox>
Prohibited and restricted content includes threats, extortion demands, hate speech, harassment, sexually explicit material, profanity, and personal information like a home address. A review carrying any of this violates policy regardless of whether the person was a customer — the test is what the review contains, not whether the underlying complaint is real.
</AnswerBox>

This type is about *how* a review is written and *what it contains*. Even a genuine customer crosses the line if they include a threat, a slur, or an extortion demand.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- "Pay me or this review stays up" — a textbook extortion demand.
- Slurs, hate speech, or harassment aimed at you or your crew.
- Doxxing — posting an employee's home address or personal details.
- Graphic profanity or sexually explicit content.

**Tell-tale signs:** an explicit demand for money or free work in exchange for removal; targeted abuse or protected-class slurs; personal information that doesn't belong in a service review.

**How to report it:** flag the review, choose the restricted/prohibited option, and — critically for extortion — save every message where the demand was made (texts, emails, DMs). Extortion in writing is one of the strongest cases there is. The full treatment is in [prohibited and restricted content reviews](/blog/google-prohibited-restricted-content-reviews).

## What about a true negative review? (None of the five)

<AnswerBox>
A true negative review from a real customer fits none of the five removable types and will not be removed. "The crew was three hours late and left a mess" is a legitimate review even at one star. Honesty and accuracy aren't violations — they're exactly what reviews are for. The right move is a calm, professional public reply, not a report.
</AnswerBox>

This is the line every honest removal effort respects. A few examples contractors often *want* gone but can't remove because they fit no category:

- **A real customer's harsh opinion.** Their experience is theirs to report, even if you think the job went fine.
- **A factually mistaken but honest review.** Being wrong about a detail isn't a violation if the person was genuinely a customer.
- **A low rating you disagree with.** Disagreement isn't a category; real-customer star ratings stand.
- **A complaint you've already fixed.** Resolution is great for your reply, but it doesn't make the original removable.

Whether a genuine, accurate negative can ever come down deserves a straight answer, which is covered in [can you remove a true negative review](/blog/can-you-remove-true-negative-review). The honest version: it can't — and trying burns the credibility you'll want for cases that actually qualify.

<Callout type="tip">
When a review is legitimate, the public reply is your best tool. Acknowledge the issue, state what you did or would do to make it right, and keep it short. Future customers read the reply as much as the review — a calm response often does more for your reputation than a removal would.
</Callout>

## Match the type, gather the proof, report once

The whole game is pattern recognition followed by evidence. Run any review through the five types: fake engagement, conflict of interest, off-topic, spam, prohibited content. The first one that clearly fits — with proof to back it — is your case. If none fit, it's legitimate, and the right response is a reply.

A precisely identified type with matching evidence is what actually gets reviews removed; vague "this is unfair" reports get denied.
