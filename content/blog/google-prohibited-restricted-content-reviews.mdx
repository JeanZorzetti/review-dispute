Most removable reviews come down to a question of authenticity — was this person really a customer, is this really about your service. Prohibited and restricted content is different. Here the question isn't *whether the complaint is true* but *how the review is written and what it contains*. A review can come from a genuine, paying, justifiably-annoyed customer and still violate policy the moment it includes a threat, a slur, an extortion demand, or your crew member's home address. This guide walks contractors through every prohibited and restricted category, what you realistically encounter in home services, and exactly how to report each one.

## What is prohibited and restricted content in a Google review?

<AnswerBox>
Prohibited and restricted content is material Google bans from reviews regardless of context — hate speech, harassment, threats and extortion, sexually explicit material, personal and confidential information, content promoting dangerous or illegal activity, and impersonation. A review carrying any of this violates policy even when the reviewer was a genuine customer with a real complaint, because the violation lives in the content itself, not in whether the underlying experience happened.
</AnswerBox>

This is the category that catches contractors off guard, because it operates on a different logic than the others. With [fake engagement or spam](/blog/spam-fake-engagement-reviews), you're proving the review isn't a real experience. With prohibited content, the experience may be entirely real — the person may have genuinely hired you and genuinely been unhappy — but the way they expressed it broke a separate rule. A customer is allowed to say your work was bad. They are not allowed to threaten you, slur your crew, post your home address, or demand money to take the review down.

<Definition term="Prohibited content">
Categories of content Google bans outright from reviews no matter the context: hate speech, harassment and bullying, threats and extortion, sexually explicit material, dangerous or illegal content, and impersonation. Because the prohibition attaches to the content itself, a review can violate it even when the reviewer was a real, paying customer.
</Definition>

<Definition term="Restricted content">
Content Google limits or removes depending on context and severity — most relevantly for contractors, profanity, obscenity, and offensive language. Unlike a single mild swear, sustained profane or abusive language in a review can qualify for removal under the restricted-content rules.
</Definition>

For where this category sits relative to the other four removable types, the overview of [the types of reviews Google removes](/blog/5-types-of-reviews-google-removes) gives the full map. The sections below cover each prohibited and restricted category and what it actually looks like on a home-services profile.

## The categories contractors actually encounter

In practice, a handful of these categories account for nearly everything a contractor will see. Here's each one, the real-world form it takes, and how to report it.

### Harassment and bullying

<AnswerBox>
Harassment is content that targets and attacks a person rather than reviewing a service — repeated abuse, intimidation, name-calling, or content designed to demean an individual. The test is feedback versus attack: criticizing your work is allowed, but personally attacking you or a named employee crosses into harassment, which violates policy and is removable.
</AnswerBox>

This is the most common prohibited-content violation in home services, and the hardest to judge because it lives next door to legitimate criticism. The dividing line is the target. "The crew was unprofessional and left a mess" is criticism of the work. "John is a worthless human being who should be ashamed to exist" is a personal attack on an individual.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- Repeated reviews from the same person targeting you or a specific employee.
- Name-calling and demeaning language aimed at a person rather than the service.
- Content clearly intended to intimidate or humiliate rather than inform other customers.

**Tell-tale signs:**

- The review attacks a named individual instead of describing a transaction.
- A pattern of escalating, repeated posts from one reviewer.
- The language is about the person's character or worth, not the quality of the job.

**Evidence to gather:** A screenshot of the abusive language with the targeted individual named, and — for repeated harassment — documentation of the pattern across multiple posts or accounts.

### Profanity and offensive language

<AnswerBox>
Google restricts profane, obscene, and offensive language in reviews, so a profanity-laden rant can qualify for removal on those grounds even when the complaint underneath is real. The stronger the case — explicit obscenities or slurs rather than a single mild swear — the more likely it comes down. Report the specific offensive language, not the negative sentiment around it.
</AnswerBox>

Contractors see this constantly: a furious customer who carpet-bombs the review with obscenities. A single mild swear probably won't get a review pulled, but a sustained profane tirade can qualify under the restricted-content rules. The key is to focus your report on the language itself, not on the fact that the review is negative.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- A review where the obscenities outnumber the actual complaint.
- Slurs or graphic language directed at you or your crew.
- Content that's offensive on its face regardless of the underlying issue.

**Tell-tale signs:**

- Explicit obscenities, not borderline word choices.
- Offensive language that adds nothing to the substance of any complaint.

**Evidence to gather:** A dated screenshot of the review with the offending language visible. Report it as a profanity/offensive-language violation, and resist the urge to argue the merits of the complaint — that's not what's at issue.

### Hate speech

<AnswerBox>
Hate speech — content attacking or demeaning people based on race, ethnicity, religion, disability, gender, sexual orientation, or other protected characteristics — is prohibited outright in Google reviews. It's removable regardless of whether the person was a customer, and it's one of the categories Google's automation is most aggressive about catching.
</AnswerBox>

Less common than profanity but unambiguous when it appears. If a review contains a slur or attacks someone based on a protected characteristic, it's a clear prohibited-content violation with no gray area.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- Slurs aimed at you, an employee, or a group.
- Content demeaning people based on a protected characteristic.

**Evidence to gather:** A screenshot of the hate speech. These cases are about the content alone — the reviewer's customer status is irrelevant.

### Personal and confidential information (doxxing)

<AnswerBox>
Posting personal or confidential information — a home address, personal phone number, license plate, financial details, or other private information about you or a crew member — violates Google's policy. Often called doxxing, it's one of the stronger removal cases because the prohibited information is visible right in the review text. Screenshot it and report it as personal information shared without consent.
</AnswerBox>

This one matters disproportionately to contractors because your crews are often individually named and locally known. A vengeful reviewer posting an employee's home address or personal cell number isn't reviewing your service — they're exposing private information, which is squarely prohibited.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- An employee's home address, personal phone, or other private details in the review.
- Financial or account information that doesn't belong in a public review.
- Any private data about an individual posted without consent.

**Evidence to gather:** An immediate dated screenshot before anything changes, with the personal information visible. Report it as a personal-information violation — and treat employee safety seriously if the doxxing is paired with threats.

### Threats, extortion, and the "pay or it stays" demand

<AnswerBox>
Threats and extortion — including the classic "pay me or this review stays up" demand — are prohibited content and among the strongest removal cases that exist. The violation is the coercion itself. Preserve every message where the threat or demand was made; a written extortion demand is close to a guaranteed removal when documented and reported correctly.
</AnswerBox>

This is the prohibited-content scenario contractors fear most and, paradoxically, the one with the best odds of removal — because extortion in writing is so clearly over the line. Someone leaves you a one-star, then messages: "I'll take it down for $500." That demand is the violation, independent of whether their original complaint had any merit.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- A "pay me or the review stays" message after a negative review.
- A demand for free work, refunds, or money in exchange for removal.
- Threats of violence or harm to you, your business, or your crew.

**Evidence to gather:** A screenshot of the review, and — critically — every message where the demand or threat was made: texts, emails, DMs. The written demand is the case. This is strong enough to warrant its own dedicated handling, and a documented extortion attempt should also be reported to the relevant authorities.

### Dangerous, illegal, and impersonation content

<AnswerBox>
Content promoting dangerous or illegal activity, and content impersonating another person or business, are both prohibited. For contractors this most often appears as a reviewer falsely posing as someone they're not — impersonating a real customer, an employee, or even your business — to lend false weight to an attack. The impersonation itself is the violation.
</AnswerBox>

These are rarer but worth recognizing. Impersonation overlaps with [conflict-of-interest and fake-engagement cases](/blog/google-review-policy-explained) when, for example, a competitor poses as a former employee. The dangerous/illegal bucket covers content promoting harm or unlawful acts, which occasionally surfaces in abusive reviews.

**Evidence to gather:** Documentation that the person isn't who they claim to be (for impersonation), or a screenshot of the dangerous or illegal content. Match the proof to the specific claim.

<Callout type="warning">
If a review is paired with a credible threat to your safety or an employee's — especially doxxing combined with threats — don't treat it as just a reputation problem. Preserve the evidence, report it to Google, and contact local law enforcement. Removal is secondary to safety.
</Callout>

## How to report prohibited content (and what stays up)

The reporting workflow for prohibited and restricted content:

1. **Capture the content immediately.** These reviews sometimes get edited or deleted by the reviewer; a dated screenshot locks in the evidence.
2. **Identify the specific category.** Harassment, profanity, hate speech, personal information, threats, or impersonation — name the exact violation, not just "abusive."
3. **Preserve any off-platform demands.** For extortion or threats, every text, email, and DM is part of the case.
4. **Report from your verified Business Profile.** Owner reports carry more weight, and you can attach the supporting evidence.
5. **Re-report and escalate if denied.** Automation may miss context-dependent violations like harassment; a human reviewer often reverses these on escalation. How those calls get made is covered in [how Google decides review removal](/blog/how-google-decides-review-removal).

What stays up: a real customer's harsh complaint that contains *no* prohibited content. "Rude, late, and overpriced — never again" is legitimate, even at one star. The negativity isn't the violation. The moment that same customer adds a threat, a slur, or your address, the prohibited content becomes removable — but the criticism alone never is.
