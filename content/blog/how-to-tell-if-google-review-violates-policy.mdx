Before you spend an hour fighting a Google review, you need to answer one question: does it actually break a rule? Google won't remove a review just because it's unfair, untrue, or stings your rating. It only removes reviews that violate its published content policies. This guide teaches contractors to self-diagnose a review the way a removal specialist would — category by category, sign by sign — so you know whether you have a real case before you lift a finger.

## Does this Google review violate policy?

<AnswerBox>
A Google review violates policy if it fits one of five categories: fake engagement (the reviewer never used your service), conflict of interest (a competitor, ex-employee, or owner), off-topic (not about your business or service), spam (ads, links, or duplicates), or prohibited/restricted content (threats, profanity, hate speech). If it fits a category and you can evidence it, it's removable. If it's an honest customer's harsh opinion, it isn't.
</AnswerBox>

The mistake almost every contractor makes is testing a review against the wrong standard. You read a one-star rant, you know it's wrong, and you assume "wrong" means "removable." It doesn't. Google is not a referee for who's right in a dispute. It's a rule enforcer with a specific rulebook — and the only question that matters is whether the review breaks one of those rules.

<Definition term="Content policy violation">
A breach of one of Google's contributed-content policies — the rules that govern what people are allowed to post in reviews. A review that violates a policy (fake, conflict of interest, off-topic, spam, or prohibited content) is eligible for removal. A review that's merely negative, inaccurate, or unflattering — but posted honestly by a real customer — does not violate policy and will not be removed.
</Definition>

So the diagnostic isn't "is this fair?" It's "which rule does this break, and can I prove it?" Work through the five categories below in order. The first one that clearly fits — with evidence to back it — is your case. If none fit, you're looking at a legitimate review, and the right move is a calm public reply, not a report. ## Category 1: Fake engagement (the reviewer was never a customer)

<AnswerBox>
Fake engagement covers reviews from people who never used your service — fabricated reviews, retaliation from non-customers, and ratings designed to manipulate your score rather than reflect a real experience. The defining test is simple: is this person in your customer records? If there's no job, no invoice, and no match in your system, you likely have a fake-engagement violation.
</AnswerBox>

This is the most common removable category for home-services businesses, and the easiest to evidence because it hinges on a fact you control: your own records.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- A one-star review from a name you've never heard of, with no address, job date, or service details.
- A review describing work you don't even offer ("they botched my pool install" when you're an HVAC company).
- A burst of low ratings in a single day, often with no text.
- A profile that has reviewed dozens of unrelated businesses in different cities in a short window.

**Tell-tale signs:**

- No identifying details a real customer would know (the crew's name, the date, the specific job).
- The reviewer's name and contact info return nothing when you search your CRM, invoicing, and job calendar.
- Generic, vague language: "terrible service, would not recommend" with nothing concrete.

**Evidence to gather:** A dated screenshot of an empty search in your customer system ("no record found"), the reviewer's profile showing scattershot review behavior, and a screenshot of the review itself with its date. The headline of your report should be one sentence: *this person is not in our customer records.*

<Callout type="warning">
A bare one-star rating with no comment is NOT automatically fake. Real customers leave star-only ratings all the time. The lack of detail is a reason to investigate — not proof. You only have a case once your records confirm the person was never a customer. For the full method of proving non-customer status, see [reporting a review not from a real customer](/blog/report-google-review-not-from-customer).
</Callout>

## Category 2: Conflict of interest (competitor, ex-employee, or owner)

<AnswerBox>
A conflict-of-interest review comes from someone with a stake in your business's reputation rather than an honest customer experience — most often a competitor posing as a customer, a fired employee, or someone with a personal grudge. The review is a weapon aimed at your rating, not feedback about a service they paid for.
</AnswerBox>

This is the violation that does the most damage to contractors, because the people behind it know exactly where to hit. A rival roofer knows which words scare homeowners. A fired installer knows your weak spots. The review reads plausibly even though it's poisoned at the source.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- A "review" from the owner or employee of a competing HVAC, roofing, or plumbing company.
- A former employee trashing you shortly after termination.
- A review timed suspiciously — right after you declined a job, lost a bid, or fired someone.
- A pattern where one profile leaves you (and only you) one star while leaving your local competitor five stars.

**Tell-tale signs:**

- No service details, but unusually "industry-aware" complaints that a normal homeowner wouldn't phrase that way.
- The reviewer's profile links to or promotes a competing business.
- Timing that lines up with a business event, not a service event.

**Evidence to gather:** Anything that establishes the relationship — the reviewer's profile showing they own or work for a competitor, employment records and termination dates for an ex-employee, screenshots of the suspicious review pattern across local businesses, and a timeline tying the review to the triggering event. Because these cases turn entirely on building the relationship case, we cover them in depth in [removing a competitor's fake review](/blog/remove-competitor-fake-review).

## Category 3: Off-topic (it's not about your service)

<AnswerBox>
An off-topic review doesn't describe an experience with your business. It might be a case of mistaken identity (a review meant for a different company), a general rant about contractors or an industry, or personal and political commentary that has nothing to do with the service you provided. Off-topic content is eligible for removal.
</AnswerBox>

Off-topic violations are underused by contractors because they feel less dramatic than fakes — but they're often the cleanest cases to prove, because the review itself contains the evidence.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- A review clearly meant for another business with a similar name in another town.
- A rant about "all roofers being crooks" with nothing specific to you.
- Commentary about your politics, your truck's bumper sticker, or a news story — not your work.
- A complaint about a third party (a supplier, a manufacturer, the city permit office) attributed to you.

**Tell-tale signs:**

- The review names a different company, location, or service you don't provide.
- The content is a general opinion, not an account of a transaction.
- The subject is something outside the service relationship entirely.

**Evidence to gather:** A screenshot highlighting the specific text that proves it's off-topic — the wrong business name, the general-rant language, the unrelated subject. For mistaken identity, a note showing the other business exists and matches the review better. The review's own words usually do most of the work.

## Category 4: Spam (ads, links, and duplicates)

<AnswerBox>
A spam review isn't genuine feedback — it's promotional content, a link drop, gibberish, or the same review duplicated across listings. The reviewer is using your profile to advertise, spread links, or game the system rather than describe your service.
</AnswerBox>

Spam is more common on profiles than contractors expect, and it's almost always removable because it has nothing to do with an actual experience.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- A "review" that's really an ad for another product or service, sometimes with a URL or phone number.
- The exact same text posted on your profile and several other local businesses.
- Bot-like gibberish, random characters, or keyword-stuffed nonsense.
- Reviews soliciting you or your customers to a third-party site.

**Tell-tale signs:**

- Contains links, promo codes, or contact info for an unrelated business.
- Identical or near-identical text appears on multiple listings.
- The content reads like marketing or automated output, not a human customer.

**Evidence to gather:** A screenshot of the promotional content or link, and — for duplicates — screenshots of the same review appearing on other businesses' profiles. Note any patterns in the reviewer's profile (lots of identical posts) as supporting proof.

## Category 5: Prohibited and restricted content (threats, profanity, hate)

<AnswerBox>
Prohibited and restricted content includes threats, extortion demands, hate speech, harassment, sexually explicit material, profanity, and personal attacks, plus content about regulated topics. A review carrying any of this violates policy regardless of whether the person was a customer.
</AnswerBox>

This category is about *how* the review is written and *what it contains*, not whether the underlying complaint is real. Even a genuine customer crosses the line if they include a threat, a slur, or an extortion demand.

**What it looks like for a contractor:**

- "Pay me or this review stays up" — a textbook extortion demand.
- Slurs, hate speech, or harassment aimed at you or your crew.
- Doxxing — posting an employee's home address or personal details.
- Graphic profanity or sexually explicit content.

**Tell-tale signs:**

- An explicit demand for money or free work in exchange for removal.
- Targeted abuse or protected-class slurs.
- Personal information that doesn't belong in a service review.

**Evidence to gather:** A screenshot of the offending content, and — critically for extortion — every message where the person made the demand (texts, emails, DMs). Extortion in writing is one of the strongest cases there is. If you're facing a "pay or I post" situation, follow the dedicated playbook in [removing extortion reviews and review threats](/blog/remove-extortion-review-threats).

## What does NOT violate policy?

<AnswerBox>
An honest negative review from a real customer does not violate policy, no matter how harsh, inaccurate, or unfair it feels. "The crew showed up late and tracked mud through my house" is a legitimate review even at one star. It can't be removed — and the right response is a calm, professional public reply.
</AnswerBox>

This is the line every honest removal effort respects. A few examples that contractors often *want* to remove but can't:

- **A real customer's harsh opinion.** Even if you think the install was fine, their experience is theirs to report.
- **A factually mistaken but honest review.** Being wrong about a detail isn't a policy violation if the person was genuinely your customer.
- **A low rating you disagree with.** Disagreement isn't a category. Star ratings from real customers stand.
- **A complaint about something you've since fixed.** Resolution is great for your reply, but it doesn't make the original review removable.

<Callout type="tip">
When a review is legitimate, your best tool is the public reply. Acknowledge the issue, state what you did or would do to make it right, and keep it short. Future customers read the reply as much as the review — a calm, fair response often does more for your reputation than a removal would.
</Callout>

## How to run the diagnostic in five minutes

Here's the workflow to settle the question fast:

1. **Read the review twice, calmly.** Strip out the emotion and look only at the facts and the wording.
2. **Search your records for the reviewer.** Name, phone, address, email. A clean miss points to fake engagement.
3. **Click the reviewer's profile.** Look for competitor links, mass-review behavior, or duplicate posts.
4. **Check the subject.** Is it about your specific service, or is it off-topic, an ad, or a rant?
5. **Scan for prohibited content.** Threats, demands, slurs, personal info.
6. **Match to a category and confirm you have evidence.** If one category fits and you can prove it, you have a case. If none fit, it's legitimate — reply instead.

The goal of this diagnostic isn't to talk yourself into a removal. It's to tell, honestly and quickly, whether you have grounds. A precisely identified violation with matching evidence is what actually gets reviews removed — vague "this is unfair" reports get denied.
