## What makes a Google review removable?

<AnswerBox>
A Google review is removable only when it violates one of Google's published content policies — it's fake, posted by someone with a conflict of interest, off-topic, spam, or contains prohibited content. A real customer's honest negative review is not removable, no matter how unfair it feels.
</AnswerBox>

This is the single most important thing for a contractor to understand, and it's where most people waste time. Google does not act as a referee for whether a complaint is "fair." It only enforces its own rulebook. If a review breaks a rule, you have grounds to get it taken down. If it doesn't, no amount of reporting, replying, or arguing will move it.

<Definition term="Policy-violating review">
A Google review that breaks one of Google's contributed-content policies — for example a fake review from a non-customer, a review posted by a competitor or former employee, a rant about a different business, a spammy link, or a review used to threaten or extort. These reviews are eligible for removal. Honest negative feedback from a real customer is not.
</Definition>

The practical filter is simple: before you spend an hour fighting a review, ask whether it breaks a rule or whether it's just a real customer who's upset. If a homeowner was genuinely unhappy with your install and left one star, that review is theirs to keep — and the right move there is a calm public reply, not a removal request. But if the "reviewer" was never your customer, is a competitor in disguise, or is using the review to pressure you for money, you have a legitimate case. ## What are the 5 types of reviews Google will remove?

<AnswerBox>
Google removes reviews in five policy categories: fake engagement, conflict of interest, off-topic, spam, and prohibited or restricted content. Most contractor cases fall into fake engagement (a non-customer) or conflict of interest (a competitor or ex-employee).
</AnswerBox>

Here is how each category maps to the real situations contractors run into, and roughly how likely a clean case is to be removed.

| Violation type | What it looks like for a contractor | Removal likelihood |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Fake engagement** | A review from someone who was never a customer; a friend-of-a-friend retaliation review; a profile with dozens of one-star reviews across unrelated businesses in one day | High — if you can show no record of the person |
| **Conflict of interest** | A competitor posing as a customer; a fired employee trashing you; the owner of a rival HVAC shop reviewing your business | High — when the relationship can be evidenced |
| **Off-topic** | A review about a different company you got confused with; a rant about contractors in general; political or personal commentary unrelated to your service | Medium to high |
| **Spam** | A review that's really an ad, contains promotional links, or is duplicated across multiple listings | Medium to high |
| **Prohibited / restricted content** | Threats, extortion ("pay me or I leave this up"), hate speech, profanity, personal attacks, or content about regulated services | High for extortion and explicit threats |

The two that matter most for home-services businesses are **fake engagement** and **conflict of interest**. Roofers, HVAC techs, and plumbers get hit hardest by competitors and disgruntled ex-employees, because those people know the business and know exactly how to sting it. If you suspect a rival is behind a review, work through our dedicated playbook on [removing a competitor's fake review](/blog/remove-competitor-fake-review).

<Callout type="warning">
What is NOT removable: a real customer's honest opinion. "The crew showed up two hours late and left nails in my driveway" is a legitimate review, even at one star. Reporting honest criticism as fake wastes your credibility with Google and can hurt future, valid reports. Reply to it instead.
</Callout>

## How do you report a fake Google review step by step?

<AnswerBox>
Report a fake review from your Google Business Profile: open the review, click the three-dot menu, choose "Report review," select the specific policy it violates, and submit. Then track the report in your Reviews dashboard and follow up if there's no decision within two weeks.
</AnswerBox>

Here's the full process, in order:

1. **Log in to the Google account that manages your Business Profile.** Search your business name on Google or go to your profile directly, then open the Reviews section.
2. **Find the offending review.** Don't act in anger — read it twice and confirm which policy it actually breaks. Picking the wrong category is a common reason for denials.
3. **Open the three-dot menu next to the review and select "Report review."** On some profiles this appears as a flag icon.
4. **Choose the most accurate violation type.** If a competitor posed as a customer, that's conflict of interest. If the person was never a customer, fake engagement. If it's about another business, off-topic. Be precise.
5. **Submit, then document everything.** Screenshot the review, the reviewer's profile, and your confirmation. Note the date you reported it.
6. **Wait, then follow up.** If you have no decision in about two weeks, escalate through Google Business Profile support (chat or the support form) and restate your case with evidence attached.

<Callout type="tip">
Report from the Business Profile Manager (your owner account), not from a random personal Google account. Reports tied to the verified business owner carry more weight than anonymous flags from the public.
</Callout>

## What evidence do you need to get a review removed?

<AnswerBox>
Strong evidence proves the reviewer was never a real customer or had a conflict of interest. Gather customer records (invoices, job tickets, CRM entries), the reviewer's profile history, screenshots, dates and timelines, and any direct communication that shows the true relationship.
</AnswerBox>

Removals are won or lost on documentation. Google's reviewers are processing enormous volumes, so the easier you make their decision, the faster and more likely the removal. Build a simple evidence file:

- **Customer records.** Search your CRM, invoicing system, and job calendar for the reviewer's name, address, and phone number. If there's no match, that's your headline: "This person is not in our customer records." Take a dated screenshot of the empty search.
- **Reviewer profile history.** Click the reviewer's name. A profile that left ten one-star reviews for unrelated businesses in a single afternoon, or that only ever reviews your competitors positively and you negatively, is a red flag worth capturing.
- **Timeline and screenshots.** Screenshot the review with its date, and note when it appeared relative to any incident (a price quote, a denied warranty claim, a firing).
- **Direct communication.** Texts, emails, or DMs where the person threatens a bad review, demands money, or reveals they're a competitor are gold. Extortion attempts in writing are among the strongest evidence you can have — see [removing extortion reviews and review threats](/blog/remove-extortion-review-threats) for handling those.

## How long does Google take to remove a fake review?

<AnswerBox>
Most well-documented reports get an initial decision within 3 to 14 days. Cases that need escalation or that Google initially denies can take 4 to 8 weeks of follow-up. The clearer the policy violation and the evidence, the faster it resolves.
</AnswerBox>

Timelines vary because there's no single queue — automated filters catch some reviews instantly, while others route to human reviewers who are slower. The variables that speed things up are all in your control: a precisely named violation, clean evidence, and a report filed from the verified owner account. The variables that slow you down are vague reports ("this is unfair"), the wrong category, and silence after a denial. For the full breakdown of what to expect and when to escalate, see [how long Google review removal takes](/blog/how-long-google-review-removal-takes).

## What do you do if Google denies the removal?

<AnswerBox>
A denial is not final. Resubmit with stronger, clearer evidence, escalate through Google Business Profile support, and if it's a legal-grade violation use Google's small-business removal tool. Most contractors who succeed do so on a second or third attempt, not the first.
</AnswerBox>

Google's first pass is often automated and blunt. A denial frequently means "the system didn't see the violation clearly," not "you're wrong." When denied:

- **Re-read the review and re-pick the category.** A surprising number of denials trace back to the wrong violation type being selected.
- **Tighten the evidence.** Lead with the single strongest fact — usually "not in our customer records" or "this is a known competitor."
- **Escalate to a human.** Use Google Business Profile support chat or the support form and attach your evidence file.
- **Use the legal/small-business removal path** for content that's defamatory, threatening, or extortionate.

The most common reasons for a denial — and exactly how to fix each one — are covered in [why Google denied your review removal](/blog/why-google-denied-review-removal). Do not let a first "no" convince you the review is permanent.

<AnswerBox>
Don't panic and don't fire back angrily. Screenshot the review and the reviewer's profile, check your customer records, identify which policy it violates, then report it from your Business Profile. If it's high-stakes or contested, hand it to a specialist.
