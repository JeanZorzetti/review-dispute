Sooner or later a contractor hears some version of this: "Refund me the full job or I'm leaving you a one-star and telling everyone you're a scammer." Sometimes it arrives by text at 11 p.m., sometimes it's said calmly to your face, sometimes it shows up as a review that's already live with a quiet follow-up: "Take care of me and I'll take it down." Whatever the packaging, it's the same move — using your reputation as a hostage to extract money or free work you don't owe. This guide is about responding to that the right way: staying calm, refusing the trade without losing your professionalism, protecting yourself with documentation, and reporting the review when it crosses the line. The removal mechanics live in a separate guide; here, the focus is what you actually say and do in the moment.

## What is review extortion?

<AnswerBox>
Review extortion is when someone threatens to post a negative review — or to keep one up — unless you give them money, free work, or a discount they aren't owed. The defining feature is the trade itself: "pay me or the one-star stays." That's fundamentally different from a complaint, where the customer wants the work fixed rather than a payout in exchange for silence. The moment the demand is tied to the review instead of to the actual job, you've left customer service and entered extortion.
</AnswerBox>

<Definition term="Review extortion">
The act of threatening to publish, or refusing to remove, a negative online review in order to coerce a business into providing money, free services, refunds, or other value the person isn't legitimately owed. The review functions as leverage, not as honest feedback.
</Definition>

It helps to name it plainly, because contractors often second-guess themselves here. When a customer is angry and demanding, the instinct is to assume you must have done something wrong and to start negotiating. Sometimes there *is* a real problem buried in there — and you should fix that. But the extortion isn't the complaint; it's the conditional threat attached to it. "The drywall finish is rough, come fix it" is a complaint. "Pay me $2,000 or I post that you ruined my house" — when the drywall is fine — is extortion. Once you can see the difference clearly, you stop negotiating against a hostage situation and start handling two separate things: a possible legitimate issue, and a threat.

## Should you pay someone threatening a bad review?

<AnswerBox>
No. Paying review extortion almost never ends it — it proves the threat works, and the same person can come back for more, or tell others that you fold under pressure. Payment also doesn't guarantee the review comes down or stays down, since the person controls whether they actually follow through. The right move is to refuse the trade politely, fix any genuine underlying problem on its own merits, document every message, and report the review if it posts. Pay to fix a real mistake; never pay to buy silence.
</AnswerBox>

The math on paying is worse than it looks. The obvious cost is the money you hand over. The hidden cost is what you teach the person: that threatening your reputation is a reliable way to get paid. There's nothing stopping them from coming back in three months with a new "issue," and nothing stopping them from mentioning to a friend in the same neighborhood that you'll cave if they push hard enough. You're not closing the problem; you're opening an account.

There's also no enforcement on their end. You pay, and they may take the review down — or edit it, or leave it, or post a new one. You have no contract, no recourse, and no leverage once the money's gone. Compare that to the alternative: you refuse calmly, you fix anything legitimately broken because it's the right thing, you keep every message, and if a retaliatory review goes up, you report it with a documented trail showing it was posted as a threat. One path makes you a mark; the other makes you a professional with a paper trail.

<Callout type="warning">
There is one distinction worth keeping straight: refunding or fixing a genuine problem is good business and is not "paying extortion." If you actually botched the job, make it right — that's customer service, and you'd do it regardless of any review. What you never do is make the payment <em>conditional on the review</em> ("I'll refund you if you take it down"). The instant the money is tied to the review outcome, you've stepped into the gray zone and handed them proof you negotiate over reviews. Fix problems because they're problems; never because a review is the gun to your head.
</Callout>

## Extortion vs. legitimate complaint: how to tell them apart

<AnswerBox>
Look at what they're actually asking for. A legitimate complaint wants the work made right — a redo, a repair, a fair adjustment for a real shortfall — and references specifics of the job. Extortion wants a payout disconnected from the work, with the review used as the lever: "give me money or this one-star stays," even when the job was fine. When the customer talks about fixing the work, it's a complaint; when they talk about the review as the bargaining chip, it's extortion.
</AnswerBox>

Most of the time you can sort it out in under a minute by listening for what's being traded. The table below lays out the tells. Real complaints and extortion can even arrive in the same message — your job is to separate the strands and respond to each correctly.

| Signal | Legitimate complaint | Review extortion |
| --- | --- | --- |
| What they want | The work fixed or a fair adjustment | A payout untied to the work |
| The review's role | Not mentioned, or a last resort if ignored | The explicit weapon: "pay or it stays up" |
| Specificity | References real details of the job | References the review itself, vague on the work |
| Proportionality | Asks for an amount tied to the actual shortfall | Demands far more than any real damage |
| Timing | Raised while trying to resolve the issue | Raised as an ultimatum, often with a deadline |
| Tone of the ask | "Come make this right" | "Make this worth my while or else" |
| Willingness to let you fix it | Wants the problem solved | Refuses fixes, only wants money |

The cleanest test: imagine you offered to fully redo the work for free, no money exchanged. A legitimate complainer is satisfied — that's what they wanted. An extortionist isn't, because the work was never the point; the payout was. When a free fix doesn't end the conversation, you're not in a customer-service situation anymore.

## What to say — and what not to say

<AnswerBox>
Keep every reply calm, brief, and professional, because anything you write may be read later by an attorney, a court, or Google's review team. Acknowledge any legitimate issue and offer to fix it on its merits, decline the conditional trade clearly but politely, and move the record to writing. Don't insult them, don't threaten back, don't beg, and never put in writing that you'll pay to remove a review. Your tone should sound like a professional who has nothing to hide — because that's exactly the impression you want on the record.
</AnswerBox>

How you phrase your response matters twice: once for resolving the situation, and once because it becomes evidence. Write every message as if it will be screenshotted and shown to a judge, an attorney, or a Google reviewer — because it might be.

**What to say:**

- *Acknowledge a real issue, separately from the threat:* "I want to make sure the work meets the standard we agreed on. If there's a defect, I'll come out and fix it."
- *Decline the trade plainly:* "I'm not able to provide a refund or payment in exchange for a review. If there's a genuine problem with the work, I'll gladly address it."
- *Set the boundary politely:* "I understand you're frustrated. I'm happy to resolve any legitimate issue, but I can't make payments based on reviews."
- *Move it to writing:* "Let's keep this in writing so we both have a clear record of what's been offered and agreed."

**What not to say** is just as important, and it overlaps heavily with the broader rules in [what not to say when replying to a bad review](/blog/what-not-to-say-replying-bad-review):

- Don't insult, mock, or call them a scammer — even when they are one. It looks bad on the record and in public.
- Don't threaten them back ("I'll sue you," "I'll report you to the police") in the heat of the moment. If legal action is warranted, your attorney handles that; don't telegraph it emotionally.
- Don't beg or panic. Desperation invites a bigger demand.
- Don't ever write "I'll pay you to take the review down" or "I'll refund you if you remove it." That single sentence can undo your whole position.

<Callout type="tip">
Move the conversation off the phone and into text or email as soon as you can — politely, by framing it as "let's keep a clear record." Verbal threats vanish; written ones don't. A simple "Can you put that in a text so I make sure I understand exactly what you're asking?" often produces a documented demand in the extortionist's own words, which is the single most valuable piece of evidence you can have.
</Callout>

## Document everything

<AnswerBox>
Save every text, email, voicemail, and screenshot related to the threat, and write down the date, time, and substance of any phone calls right after they happen. Documentation protects you on two fronts: it's the record an attorney or law enforcement needs if the matter turns legal, and it's the evidence that strengthens a removal request to Google by showing the review was posted as a threat rather than as honest feedback. The contractor with a clean paper trail is the one in control.
</AnswerBox>

Documentation is the quiet superpower in any extortion situation, and it costs you nothing but a habit. The goal is a clean, chronological record that tells the whole story without you having to remember details under pressure later.

Capture, at minimum:

- **Every written message** — texts, emails, social media DMs, voicemails. Screenshot texts and DMs (they can be deleted) and back them up somewhere outside the phone.
- **A call log** — after any phone conversation, immediately jot down the date, time, who said what, and the specific demand. Memory fades; notes don't.
- **The original job record** — your contract, invoice, change orders, before/after photos, and signed completion. This is what proves the work was actually fine when they claim it wasn't.
- **The review itself** — screenshot it the moment it posts, including the reviewer name and date, in case they edit or delete it.

This record does double duty. If the situation ever becomes a legal matter, your attorney starts from a complete file instead of your recollection. And if you pursue removal, a documented demand — "pay me or the review stays" — is powerful evidence that the review isn't a genuine customer account.

## When it crosses into illegal coercion

<AnswerBox>
A complaint becomes potential extortion or coercion when someone demands money or services under threat of harming your business reputation, with no legitimate basis for the demand. In many U.S. states that can be a crime. We're contractors and reputation specialists, not attorneys, so treat any genuine threat as a reason to preserve every message and talk to a lawyer or local authorities. Whatever the legal label, the practical response is the same — document, refuse the trade, and report the review.
</AnswerBox>

There's a point where this stops being a tough customer and starts being a potential crime. Broadly, demanding money or value under threat of harm — including harm to your business reputation — can constitute extortion or coercion under many state laws. We're not lawyers, and we're not going to pretend this article is legal advice; the specifics vary by state and by exactly what was said. What we can say plainly is that if a demand feels like a genuine threat rather than a frustrated customer, that's your signal to stop handling it casually: preserve everything and get real advice from an attorney or your local authorities.

You don't need to figure out the legal label to protect yourself. Whether it ends up being called a business dispute or coercion, your moves don't change — stay calm, refuse to pay for silence, keep every message, and report the review. The documentation you've been building is exactly what a lawyer or law enforcement officer would ask for first.
