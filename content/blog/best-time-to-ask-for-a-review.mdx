You can write the perfect review request, set up the perfect one-tap link, and ask every single customer — and still collect far fewer reviews than you should, for one reason: bad timing. A review request sent a week after the job lands on a customer who's moved on. The same request, handed over thirty seconds after they said "wow, this looks great," gets a yes. Timing isn't a minor tweak on top of asking; it's often the difference between a 10% response rate and a 40% one. This guide breaks down the timing science for contractors — the peak-satisfaction moment, same-day versus next-day, the underrated power of a service-recovery win, and how channel and season change the calculus.

## When is the best time to ask a customer for a review?

<AnswerBox>
The best time to ask is the moment of peak satisfaction — right after the job is finished, the customer has seen the result, and they've said or signaled they're happy. For most trades that's at the final walkthrough or immediately after. This moment captures the customer's emotion at its strongest, when leaving a review feels like a natural extension of how they already feel. Wait even a few days and the glow fades; the same customer who would have given you five stars on completion day often never gets around to it later.
</AnswerBox>

Reviews are emotional. A customer leaves one when they feel something strongly enough to act — gratitude, relief, delight. Your job is to make the ask while that feeling is at its peak, because the feeling is the fuel. The completion of a job done well is almost always that peak: the new system is running, the leak is gone, the room looks transformed, and the customer is standing there genuinely pleased. That's the window.

<Definition term="Peak-satisfaction moment">
The point in the customer journey when a customer's positive emotion about your work is at its highest — typically right after job completion, the final walkthrough, or the resolution of an urgent problem. Asking for a review at this moment produces the highest response rate because the motivation to share is strongest and the experience is freshest in the customer's mind.
</Definition>

The mistake almost every contractor makes is treating the review request as paperwork to handle later. The invoice goes out, the crew packs up, and the plan is to "send a review link this week." That week becomes two, the customer's enthusiasm cools, and a five-star evaporates. The fix is to treat the ask as the last step of the job itself — part of the close, not an afterthought. ## Same-day vs. next-day: which converts better?

<AnswerBox>
Same-day, in person, at job completion converts best — the customer is at their happiest and you're standing right there to make the ask. A next-day follow-up text or email is the ideal backup for customers who agreed but didn't follow through, or jobs that ended without a clean in-person moment. The strongest approach uses both: ask in person at completion, then send one polite follow-up the next day if no review has appeared. That combination captures the eager-right-now customers and the I-meant-to ones.
</AnswerBox>

These aren't competing options — they're a one-two punch. Here's how each plays its role:

**Same-day, in person.** This is your highest-converting moment. The emotion is at its peak and you have the customer's attention. The move is simple: at the walkthrough, after they've expressed they're happy, say something like "I'm really glad you're happy with it — would you mind leaving us a quick review? I can text you the link right now." Then text the direct link on the spot so they have it in hand. The in-person ask plus the immediate link is the gold standard.

**Next-day follow-up.** Plenty of customers fully intend to review you and simply get pulled into their day. A single polite follow-up the next day — "Thanks again for having us out yesterday; here's that review link if you have a minute" — recovers a meaningful share of them without being pushy. One follow-up is the sweet spot; a third or fourth nag starts to annoy and can backfire.

The trap to avoid: relying on the follow-up *instead* of the in-person ask because it feels less awkward. The delayed-only approach leaves your best moment on the table. Ask at the peak, then follow up once. ## The service-recovery win: an overlooked prime moment

<AnswerBox>
A service-recovery win is one of the best moments to ask for a review. When you've solved an urgent or stressful problem — restored heat in winter, stopped an active leak, fixed something another company botched — the customer feels intense relief and gratitude. That emotional peak is often stronger than the satisfaction from a routine job, which makes the customer genuinely eager to share what happened. Ask while the relief is fresh, the same day you resolve the problem.
</AnswerBox>

Contractors tend to think of reviews as something you collect after smooth, routine jobs. But some of the most powerful reviews come from the moments that started badly and ended well. A homeowner whose furnace died at 11 p.m. in January and who you got running again feels something far more intense than a customer who had a routine tune-up. That intensity is exactly what fuels a glowing, detailed review.

The same is true when you rescue a job another company messed up. The customer arrives at you frustrated and skeptical, and when you deliver, the contrast makes the relief enormous. Those customers often write the most persuasive reviews of all — the ones that mention how you saved the day. The key is timing: ask while the relief is still fresh, the same day you resolve it, before the stress of the original problem fades into a distant memory.

<Callout type="tip">
After an emergency fix, the moment the customer exhales and says "thank you so much, I was panicking" is your cue. That's a peak-satisfaction moment as strong as any. "I'm really glad we got it sorted for you. If you've got a minute, a quick review would mean a lot — it helps other folks find us when they're in the same bind." It converts because it's true and the gratitude is real.
</Callout>

## Moment-by-moment: ask or wait?

<AnswerBox>
Ask when the customer is happy and the work is visibly complete; wait when the job is unfinished, the customer is stressed or upset, or there's an open issue. The simplest rule: only ask after a customer has had a positive experience and the work is done. Never ask in the middle of a problem, before the result is visible, or while a complaint is unresolved — those asks either fail or produce the kind of review you don't want.
</AnswerBox>

This table maps the common moments in a contractor's job to whether it's time to ask or hold off.

| Moment | Ask or wait? | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Final walkthrough, customer is happy | **Ask** | Peak satisfaction; the ideal moment |
| Right after an emergency fix and visible relief | **Ask** | Intense gratitude makes for a strong review |
| Next day, no review yet from a happy customer | **Ask (one follow-up)** | Recovers the "I meant to" customers |
| Mid-project, work still in progress | **Wait** | Result isn't visible; satisfaction not formed yet |
| Customer raised a concern you haven't resolved | **Wait** | Resolve it first; asking now invites a negative |
| Job done but customer seemed neutral or rushed | **Ask, lightly** | Still worth a low-pressure ask; respect the energy |
| Weeks after completion, never asked | **Ask once, gently** | Late but recoverable; then fix your process |
| Customer is visibly upset or mid-complaint | **Wait** | Address the issue; never ask for a review to soften a complaint |

One row deserves emphasis: when a customer has an unresolved concern, you wait — you fix it first. Asking everyone the same way is required by Google's rules, but that's about *who* you ask, not *when*. Resolving an open issue before asking is just good timing and good service, not gating. The distinction between fair timing and prohibited gating is spelled out in [is it against Google's rules to ask for reviews](/blog/is-it-against-google-rules-to-ask-for-reviews). The short version: ask everyone, but ask each person at a good moment for them — and a moment when they're mid-complaint isn't a good one for anybody.

## Seasonal and channel timing

<AnswerBox>
Seasonal timing matters for trades with busy and slow stretches: in your peak season you're completing the most jobs, so it's the most important time to have a tight ask process running — that's when review volume can grow fastest. Channel timing matters too: texts get read and acted on within minutes, making them best for the same-day ask, while email suits a slightly more detailed follow-up. Match the channel to the moment, and don't let your busiest season pass without collecting the reviews it could generate.
</AnswerBox>

**Seasonal.** If you're an HVAC contractor, your summer and the first cold snap are when you complete the most jobs and resolve the most emergencies — which means they're when the most reviewable moments happen. Roofers see this after storms; landscapers in spring; plumbers year-round but with weather-driven spikes. The lesson is to make sure your ask process is locked in *before* the busy season hits, so the flood of completed jobs translates into a flood of reviews instead of a flood of missed chances. A steady drip also matters for recency, so spreading requests across the year keeps your newest review fresh even in slow months.

**Channel.** Different channels suit different moments:

- **Text** is the workhorse for the same-day ask. It's read within minutes, the link is one tap away, and it matches the immediacy of the completion moment. This is your primary channel.
- **Email** suits a slightly longer next-day follow-up where you can add a sentence of thanks and context. It's also useful for customers who prefer email or for B2B clients.
- **In person plus a printed leave-behind or QR code** reinforces the digital ask — the customer hears it, gets the text, and has a card or code as a backup.

The dominant factor is still *when* relative to the job, not the channel itself. A perfectly-timed text beats a poorly-timed email every time. But matching the channel to the moment — text for the immediate ask, email for the follow-up — squeezes out the extra response.
