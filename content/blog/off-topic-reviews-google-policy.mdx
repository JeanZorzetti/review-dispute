Some of the most frustrating Google reviews aren't even about you. A homeowner blasts you for a manufacturer's defect you didn't cause. Someone reviews "the rude staff" at a business three towns over that happens to share your name. A stranger uses your profile to vent about politics or contractors in general. These off-topic reviews drag down your rating for things that have nothing to do with the work you actually did — and unlike honest criticism, they're squarely removable under Google's policy. This guide explains exactly what counts as off-topic, walks through the contractor scenarios you'll recognize, and shows you how to argue an off-topic report so it lands.

## What is an off-topic Google review?

<AnswerBox>
An off-topic Google review is one that doesn't describe an actual experience with your business. It includes reviews meant for a different company (mistaken identity), general rants about an industry, personal or political commentary, and complaints about a third party like a supplier or the permit office. Because a review is supposed to reflect a real transaction with the specific business, content that isn't about your service is off-topic and eligible for removal.
</AnswerBox>

Off-topic is one of the five removable categories in Google's contributed-content policy, and conceptually it's the simplest: a review of your business should be about your business. When the content drifts into something else — a different company, a general gripe, a political tangent, a problem you didn't cause — it stops being a review in any useful sense and becomes noise that misleads the people reading it. That's why Google's policy treats it as removable, and why it sits alongside fake engagement, conflict of interest, spam, and prohibited content in [the five types of reviews Google removes](/blog/5-types-of-reviews-google-removes).

<Definition term="Off-topic content">
Content in a review that does not describe an experience with the business being reviewed. This covers mistaken-identity reviews intended for another company, general commentary about an industry or topic, personal or political statements, and complaints about parties other than the business. Off-topic content is eligible for removal because it provides no accurate signal about the business to prospective customers.
</Definition>

The mental test is short: *Is this review about a transaction with my specific business?* If yes, even a harsh review is on-topic and stays. If no — if it's about someone else, something else, or nothing in particular — you may have an off-topic violation. For how Google weighs that judgment on its end, see [how Google decides review removal](/blog/how-google-decides-review-removal).

## The four common forms of off-topic reviews

Off-topic shows up in four recognizable shapes for contractors. Here they are side by side, then in detail.

| Form | What it looks like | What proves it | How to frame the report |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Mistaken identity** | Names a different company, location, or a service you don't offer | The wrong name/service in the review text itself | "This review names a different business — we are not [X]" |
| **General industry rant** | "All roofers are crooks" with no specifics about you | Absence of any described transaction | "This is general commentary, not an account of our service" |
| **Personal / political** | Your politics, a bumper sticker, a news story, a grudge | Subject is your opinions, not your work | "This is about [topic], not an experience with our business" |
| **Third-party complaint** | Blames you for a manufacturer, supplier, or city issue | The criticized thing wasn't your service or under your control | "This concerns [third party], not work we performed" |

### Form 1: Mistaken identity (the review is for another business)

This is the cleanest off-topic case there is, because the review almost always names the wrong company itself. You're an electrician named "Summit Electric" and a one-star rolls in complaining about a plumbing job by "Summit Plumbing" two counties over. Or the reviewer describes a storefront and walk-in hours when you're a mobile service. The review wasn't meant for you at all.

**What proves it:** the review's own text — the wrong business name, a service you don't provide, a location you don't serve, hours you don't keep. Screenshot it and, if you can, note the actual business it fits (a similar-named company nearby strengthens the case).

### Form 2: General industry rant

Here the reviewer isn't describing anything you did — they're venting about contractors, roofers, or "companies like this" in the abstract. "Typical contractor, never trust them" with zero specifics is opinion about a category, not feedback about a transaction. There's no job, no date, no crew, no service described.

**What proves it:** the *absence* of any described experience. Point out that the review contains no account of work performed — no service, no date, no interaction — only a generalized opinion.

### Form 3: Personal or political content

Sometimes a stranger uses your profile as a soapbox. They saw a flag on your truck, read something about your company in the news, or just have a personal grudge, and the "review" is about that — your politics, your beliefs, your personality — not your work. None of it describes a service experience.

**What proves it:** the subject matter. Highlight that the review discusses your opinions, a news item, or a personal matter rather than any work you did for the reviewer.

### Form 4: Third-party complaint

This one is subtler. The reviewer is genuinely unhappy, but about something that wasn't your service: a manufacturer's defective unit, a supplier's backorder, a city permit delay, an HOA decision. The frustration is real; it's just aimed at the wrong target. If your business didn't perform and didn't control the thing being criticized, the review isn't really about your service.

**What proves it:** evidence that the criticized issue was a third party's responsibility, not yours — the manufacturer warranty, the supplier's delay notice, the permit timeline. Be careful and honest here: if any part of the complaint *is* about your work (how you communicated, whether you set expectations), that part may be legitimate.

<Callout type="warning">
The third-party form is the easiest to overreach on. If a customer blames you for a manufacturer defect but also says your crew never returned their calls, the call-back complaint is on-topic and legitimate — you can't make the whole review vanish by pointing at the manufacturer. Argue off-topic only when the review genuinely isn't about your service.
</Callout>

## How do you argue that a review is off-topic?

<AnswerBox>
Argue off-topic by quoting the exact text that proves the review isn't about your service, then stating plainly that no transaction with your business is described. Lead with one clear sentence — "this review names a different company" or "this describes no experience with us" — pick the off-topic category when you flag it, and attach a dated screenshot highlighting the off-topic content. Let the review's own words carry the case.
</AnswerBox>

Off-topic reports win on clarity, not volume. Because the evidence is usually inside the review itself, your job is to make the off-topic nature impossible to miss:

1. **Identify the form.** Mistaken identity, general rant, personal/political, or third-party complaint. Knowing which one shapes your single sentence.
2. **Quote the smoking-gun text.** The wrong business name, the "all contractors are crooks" line, the political tangent, the manufacturer reference. Highlight it in your screenshot.
3. **State the absence.** For general rants especially, name what's missing: "no service, date, or interaction with our business is described anywhere in this review."
4. **Lead with one sentence.** Open the report with the clearest version of the violation. "This review describes a plumbing company; we are an electrical contractor and never performed this work."
5. **Flag the off-topic category and submit from the verified owner account.** Choosing the precise category matters — don't file an off-topic review as "spam."

<Callout type="tip">
Write your case as a single sentence before you submit: "This review is off-topic because [specific reason from the text]." If you can't fill that in cleanly using the review's own words, it may not actually be off-topic — reconsider before reporting.
</Callout>

## What is NOT off-topic (even if you wish it were)

<AnswerBox>
Honest feedback about your actual service is not off-topic, no matter how negative. If the reviewer describes a real transaction with your business — your crew, your timeline, your work — then it's on-topic and stays up, even if it's harsh or you think it's unfair. Off-topic is about subject matter, not tone. A bad review about your real work is exactly what reviews are for.
</AnswerBox>

The temptation with off-topic is to stretch it over reviews you simply don't like. Resist that. These are on-topic and will stay:

- **A harsh but real complaint about your work.** "The install took two days longer than quoted and they left debris" is about your service. On-topic.
- **A review you think is exaggerated.** Disagreeing with the framing doesn't make it off-topic if it describes your actual job.
- **Criticism of your communication or pricing.** How you quoted, scheduled, or communicated is part of the service experience — on-topic.
- **A complaint about a sub you hired.** If you brought the subcontractor onto the job, their work was part of your service. That's on-topic, unlike a true third-party defect.

The honest line is the same one that runs through all of Google's policy: subject matter that's genuinely about your service stays, and stretching "off-topic" to cover legitimate criticism just gets your report denied. In each case, the words on the screen make the argument for you — your job is just to point at them clearly and pick the right category.
