## Why isn't my Google Business Profile showing up?

<AnswerBox>
Your Google Business Profile isn't showing up because of one or more specific, diagnosable issues — most commonly an incomplete profile, wrong or too-generic categories, too few reviews or a low rating, inconsistent business information (NAP) across the web, no recent activity, an unverified or suspended profile, or simply being too far from the searcher. For contractors, weak review signals are one of the most frequent and most controllable causes. Ranking isn't random and you can't pay to fix it — you diagnose the cause and correct it.
</AnswerBox>

Before getting into causes, it helps to understand the frame Google uses. Its local algorithm weighs three things: **relevance** (how well your profile matches the search), **distance** (how close you are to the searcher), and **prominence** (how established and trusted your business is). Almost every ranking problem is a weakness in one of these three. Distance you can barely influence. Relevance you tune through your profile setup. Prominence — where reviews live — is the lever with the longest handle. The diagnostic below is organized so you fix the foundation first, then build the prominence that actually moves you up.

<Definition term="Prominence">
The component of Google's local ranking algorithm that reflects how well-known, established, and trusted a business is. Prominence draws on information from across the web — links, articles, directories — and, critically for contractors, on review signals: the count, average rating, recency, and keywords of your Google reviews, plus your response rate. Prominence is the ranking factor you can most directly grow through consistent reputation work.
</Definition>

## The technical foundation: is the profile even eligible to rank?

<AnswerBox>
Before reviews or optimization matter, your profile has to be eligible to rank at all — which means it must be claimed, verified, complete, and free of suspensions. An unverified, suspended, or skeletal profile won't rank no matter how good your work or how many reviews you have. These foundational issues are the first thing to rule out because they override every other signal.
</AnswerBox>

These are the gating issues. If any of them is true, fixing reviews won't help until you clear it.

### Unverified or unclaimed profile

If you've never claimed and verified your Business Profile, Google has little reason to feature it. An unverified listing may appear faintly or not at all in competitive searches. Claim it through Google Business Profile and complete verification (by postcard, phone, email, or video, depending on what Google offers you). This is non-negotiable groundwork.

### Suspended profile

A suspended profile vanishes from search entirely. Suspensions happen for policy issues — keyword-stuffed business names, a fake or virtual address, ineligible service-area setups, or a history of guideline violations. If your profile suddenly disappeared, check for a suspension notice and file for reinstatement after correcting whatever triggered it. Note that legitimately disputing fake reviews does *not* cause suspensions, despite a persistent myth to the contrary.

### Incomplete profile

A half-filled profile gives Google little to match against. Missing categories, hours, a description, services, service areas, or photos all weaken relevance and prominence. Google has stated that complete and accurate information helps your business match more searches. Fill every field — completeness is free and foundational.

## The relevance problem: does Google understand what you do?

<AnswerBox>
A relevance problem means Google doesn't clearly understand what your business does or where you serve, so it doesn't match you to the searches that should find you. The usual culprits are a wrong or too-generic primary category, missing secondary categories, an undefined service area, or thin profile content. Fixing categories and service areas is one of the fastest ranking improvements a contractor can make.
</AnswerBox>

Relevance is about matching. If a homeowner searches "emergency roof repair" and your primary category is the vague "Contractor" instead of "Roofing Contractor," you're competing at a disadvantage for the exact searches you want.

### Wrong or duplicate categories

Your primary category is one of the strongest relevance signals you have. Choose the most specific category that describes your core service ("HVAC Contractor," "Plumber," "Roofing Contractor"), and add accurate secondary categories for the other services you offer. Avoid choosing categories that don't truly apply — that confuses the match rather than broadening it.

### Duplicate listings

If two profiles exist for the same business — maybe an old one you forgot about — they split your signals and can suppress both. Search for duplicates and ask Google to merge or remove the extra so all your prominence consolidates into one listing.

### Undefined or wrong service area

Service-area businesses (which most contractors are) need their service areas set correctly. If your areas are missing, too narrow, or absurdly wide, your relevance for nearby searches suffers. Set the cities or regions you actually serve.

## The reputation problem: too few reviews, low rating, or no recent activity

<AnswerBox>
The most common controllable reason a contractor's profile won't rank is weak review signals: too few reviews, a low average rating, or no recent reviews. Reviews are the largest part of the prominence factor you can directly influence, so a thin or stale review profile leaves you outranked by competitors who actively earn reviews — even when your actual work is better. Building a steady flow of genuine, recent reviews is the highest-leverage ranking work most contractors can do.
</AnswerBox>

Once the technical foundation and relevance are solid, reputation is what separates the profiles that rank from those that don't. This is where most contractors are quietly losing.

### Too few reviews

Volume is a prominence signal, and it's relative to your competitors. If the roofers ranking above you average 90 reviews and you have 14, that gap alone can explain the position. There's no magic number — the goal is to out-earn the businesses competing for the same searches. The fix is unglamorous and permanent: ask every satisfied customer for a review, every job, forever.

### Low average rating

A low average undercuts prominence and, just as damaging, kills the click even when you do rank. Raise it the honest way — through volume and quality — not by trying to scrub legitimate criticism. A high average built on many real reviews is durable; one built on a tiny sample is fragile.

### No recent activity

A pile of reviews from two years ago tells Google your business *was* active. A steady drip of fresh reviews tells it you *are*. Recency is one of the most underrated ranking signals. A profile earning a few reviews every month routinely outranks a stale one with a higher lifetime total. Never let your review flow stall.

### Thin or keyword-less reviews

When customers naturally describe the work — "fixed our furnace at 11pm," "great roof leak repair" — those words add relevance to your profile. You can't and must never script reviews, but high volume tends to surface this language on its own. A profile of one-word reviews carries less signal than one full of specific, descriptive text.

For the full mechanics of how each of these review signals feeds the algorithm, see [how Google reviews affect local ranking](/blog/how-google-reviews-affect-local-ranking).

If your technical foundation is solid but you're still buried, reviews are almost certainly the gap. Add a one-tap review request to your job-close checklist and respond to every review you have. Count and recency are the two signals with the most headroom for most contractors, and they compound month over month.
</Callout>

## The consistency problem: NAP and citations

<AnswerBox>
A consistency problem means your business name, address, and phone number (NAP) appear differently across the web — different spellings, old addresses, outdated phone numbers — which erodes Google's confidence in your information and your prominence. Cleaning up NAP across your website, directories, and citations is tedious but meaningfully strengthens local ranking.
</AnswerBox>

<Definition term="NAP consistency">
NAP stands for Name, Address, and Phone number. NAP consistency means these details are identical everywhere your business appears online — your website, Google Business Profile, Yelp, industry directories, and local citations. When they conflict (an old phone number here, an abbreviated street name there), Google trusts your data less, which can hold down your ranking.
</Definition>

Contractors accumulate inconsistencies over years — a number from a previous office, a suite that got dropped, "Street" spelled out one place and "St." another. Audit your top citations and make every instance match your Business Profile exactly. It won't move you to the top alone, but inconsistency quietly caps how high you can climb.

## The distance problem: you may simply be too far

<AnswerBox>
Distance is the one factor you can't change: the closer a business is to the searcher, the more likely it ranks for that search. If you're testing your ranking from outside your immediate service radius, or competing against businesses physically closer to a dense customer area, distance may explain the gap regardless of your reviews or profile quality. Focus your prominence efforts so you win the searches within your realistic radius.
</AnswerBox>

A common false alarm: a contractor searches their service from home, sees a competitor across town ranking first, and assumes something's broken. Often nothing is — that competitor is just closer to where most of those searches originate. You can't relocate to chase rankings, but you can make sure that within your genuine service radius, your relevance and prominence are strong enough to win. ## The diagnostic checklist: symptom → cause → fix

Run through this table top to bottom. The order matters — clear the foundation issues before investing in reviews, because reviews can't rank a profile that isn't eligible to be seen.

| Symptom | Likely cause | Fix |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Profile doesn't appear at all, anywhere | Unverified or suspended | Claim and verify; if suspended, fix the violation and request reinstatement |
| Disappeared suddenly | Suspension or guideline issue | Check for a suspension notice; correct and appeal |
| Appears for your name but not for services | Wrong/generic primary category | Set the most specific category; add accurate secondaries |
| Two listings show up | Duplicate profile | Request merge or removal of the duplicate |
| Ranks at home but not across town | Distance (often normal) | Focus prominence on winning your realistic radius |
| Outranked by lighter competitors | Too few or stale reviews | Earn genuine reviews every job; keep them recent |
| Ranked but few clicks | Low average rating | Raise the average through volume and quality |
| Slow, gradual slide in position | No recent activity / NAP drift | Resume steady reviews and posts; clean up NAP |
| Strong reviews but still buried | Incomplete profile or NAP inconsistency | Fill every field; align NAP across all citations |
| Average dragged by an obvious fake | Policy-violating review | Pursue targeted removal of the violation only |

This diagnostic is one piece of a larger system. Earning reviews, responding, monitoring, and removing genuine violations all work together — see the pillar guide on [online reputation management for contractors](/blog/online-reputation-management-for-contractors) for how ranking fits alongside the rest.

<Callout type="warning">
Don't try to fix a ranking problem by buying reviews or stuffing keywords into your business name. Both violate Google's guidelines and can get your profile suspended — turning a ranking problem into a complete disappearance. The honest path is slower but it's the only one that holds.
</Callout>

## Putting it together: the order of operations

Diagnosis is only useful if you act in the right sequence. Here's the order that works:

1. **Confirm eligibility.** Claim, verify, and rule out any suspension. Nothing else matters until the profile can be seen.
2. **Complete the profile.** Fill every field — categories, services, service areas, hours, description, photos. Completeness is free relevance and prominence.
3. **Fix categories and duplicates.** Set the most specific primary category, add accurate secondaries, and eliminate duplicate listings.
4. **Align your NAP.** Make your name, address, and phone identical across your website and every directory.
5. **Build reviews relentlessly.** Add a one-tap request to every job close, respond to every review, and keep the flow recent. This is the engine of long-term ranking.
6. **Re-check quarterly against competitors.** Ranking is relative; audit the businesses beating you and out-earn them on the signals you control.

Most contractors who feel invisible on Google are missing two or three items on this list — usually a category problem plus a thin, stale review profile. Fix the foundation, then pour your ongoing energy into reviews, and the profile climbs.
