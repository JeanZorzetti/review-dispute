## How many 5-star reviews to offset a 1-star?

<AnswerBox>
There's no universal number — it depends entirely on how many reviews you already have and what average you want to get back to. On a thin profile, a single 1-star is devastating and can take many 5-stars to recover from. On a deep profile, the same 1-star barely moves your average and takes only a handful of 5-stars to absorb. The reason is simple arithmetic: your rating is the mean of all your reviews, so each one's influence shrinks as your total grows. The table below gives you the specific number for your situation.
</AnswerBox>

The instinct is to look for a magic ratio — "five 5-stars cancel one 1-star," for example. That's wrong because it ignores the only variable that matters: your existing review count. A 1-star does very different damage to a profile with 10 reviews versus one with 500, so the recovery effort is wildly different too. To get a real answer, you have to understand how the average is actually calculated.

<Definition term="Average star rating">
The arithmetic mean of all of a business's star ratings — the sum of every star value divided by the total number of reviews. Google displays this mean (rounded) as your star rating. Because it's a simple average, every individual review counts equally in the sum but represents a smaller fraction of the whole as your review count rises, which is why each review's impact on your displayed rating shrinks over time.
</Definition>

That definition is the entire game. Once you see your rating as a fraction — total stars over total reviews — the strategy becomes obvious: the bigger the denominator, the less any single review can move the number. Let's prove it with real cases.

## The math, worked out

<AnswerBox>
To find your average, add up all your star values and divide by your number of reviews. A 1-star drags that average down by an amount that depends on your review count: huge when the count is small, tiny when it's large. To offset it, you add 5-star reviews until the average climbs back to your target — and because each new review is a smaller slice of a growing total, the number you need scales with how deep your profile already is.
</AnswerBox>

Take two contractors, each sitting at a clean 5.0 average when a single 1-star lands.

**Thin profile (5 reviews):** Five 5-stars sum to 25 stars over 5 reviews = 5.0. Add a 1-star: now 26 stars over 6 reviews = **4.33**. That one review cratered the average by two-thirds of a star. To climb back near 4.8, this contractor needs to add roughly **nine** more 5-star reviews (26 + 45 = 71 stars over 15 reviews = 4.73). One bad review, nine good ones to recover.

**Deep profile (200 reviews):** Two hundred 5-stars sum to 1,000 stars over 200 reviews = 5.0. Add a 1-star: now 1,001 stars over 201 reviews = **4.98**. The displayed rating doesn't even budge. To erase the dent entirely, this contractor needs about **three** more 5-stars. Same 1-star, a tiny fraction of the effort.

Same single bad review. One contractor needs nine 5-stars to recover; the other needs three and probably won't bother. That gap — nine versus three — is the whole reason volume matters and the whole reason early reviews carry outsized weight.

## Why early reviews matter most

<AnswerBox>
Early reviews matter most because each review's weight in your average is inversely proportional to your total review count. Your first ten reviews swing your rating dramatically; your five-hundredth barely registers. That means your earliest reviews disproportionately set your reputation, and a bad review early on does outsized, lasting damage. The strategic implication is to build review volume as fast as possible at the start, when each review counts the most.
</AnswerBox>

This is the most counterintuitive piece of the math and the most important. The weight of any single review is roughly one divided by your total review count. With 5 reviews, each one carries 20% of your rating. With 500, each carries 0.2%. So:

- **Your first reviews are your foundation.** They set the average that everything later adjusts. A bad review among your first ten is far more damaging — and far harder to dilute — than one among your second hundred.
- **The window of vulnerability is early.** A new or low-volume contractor is most exposed exactly when a fake or competitor review can do the most harm. This is when one bad review can knock you under a prospect's mental cutoff.
- **Speed compounds.** The faster you build volume early, the faster you cross into the zone where individual reviews stop mattering. Every week you delay leaves you in the high-vulnerability zone longer.

The practical takeaway is to attack review volume hardest at the beginning, not after you've been burned. A one-tap review request built into every job close, from day one, is how you race out of the danger zone. ## Why removing a fake 1-star beats out-reviewing it

<AnswerBox>
If a 1-star violates Google's policy — a fake from a non-customer, a competitor attack, or an off-topic rant — removing it is far more efficient than offsetting it with new reviews. Removal instantly restores your average to exactly where it would have been, while out-reviewing requires earning several genuine 5-stars over weeks or months. The catch is that removal only works on policy-violating reviews. An honest 1-star from a real customer can never be removed — only offset.
</AnswerBox>

Here's where the math points to a clear decision. Imagine you're a contractor with 25 reviews, and a fake one-star from a competitor lands. Your two options:

- **Out-review it.** Earn roughly four genuine 5-stars to drag your average back up. That's four happy customers you have to ask, who have to follow through — realistically weeks of work, and your rating sits dented the whole time.
- **Remove it.** If it's a genuine policy violation, getting it taken down restores your average to its pre-review state immediately. One action, full recovery, no waiting.

For a *fake* review, removal wins on every axis — speed, effort, and completeness. Why earn four reviews to cancel a review that shouldn't exist in the first place? The full process for identifying and removing a policy-violating one-star is in the pillar guide on [how to remove a fake Google review](/blog/how-to-remove-fake-google-review).

<Callout type="warning">
This only applies to reviews that actually violate Google's policy. If the 1-star is from a real customer who genuinely had a bad experience, removal is not an option — Google protects honest reviews, and trying to remove them wastes effort and can risk your profile. For honest negatives, the math leaves you exactly one path: earn more 5-stars and respond well. Be honest with yourself about which kind you're dealing with before you choose.
</Callout>

## Putting the math to work

The arithmetic gives you a clear, three-part playbook for any one-star:

1. **Find your number.** Use the table to see how many 5-stars it would take to offset the review at your current count. That number tells you how big a problem you actually have — small if you're deep, large if you're thin.
2. **Diagnose the review.** Is it an honest customer or a policy violation? This single question decides your strategy. Honest means offset; violation means remove.
3. **Act on the faster path.** For honest negatives, launch a review-earning push and reply professionally. For policy violations, pursue removal — it's the faster, cleaner fix that restores your average in one move.

Underneath all three is the same durable truth: build volume relentlessly. A deep, recent review profile makes the offset number small, makes honest negatives harmless, and makes you resilient to the next bad day. The math rewards contractors who treat reviews as an always-on habit, not a fire drill.
