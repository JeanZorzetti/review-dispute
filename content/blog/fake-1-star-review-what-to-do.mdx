It's the gut-punch every contractor knows: you glance at your phone and there it is — a fresh one-star review from a name you don't recognize, tanking the rating you worked years to build. Your first instinct is to fire back. Don't. The first 24 hours decide whether this becomes a minor cleanup or a public mess. This is the calm, step-by-step playbook for the moment a fake one-star lands — what to do, in what order, and what to never do.

## Just got a fake 1-star review — what do I do first?

<AnswerBox>
Do not respond yet. First, screenshot the review and the reviewer's full profile before anything can change. Then step away from the keyboard, read the review twice without reacting, and check whether it actually violates a Google policy. Only after documenting and diagnosing should you choose between a calm public reply, a policy report, or both. The order matters: react first and you'll often make it worse.
</AnswerBox>

The reason the first move is "do nothing public" is simple: emotion is the enemy here, and everything you do in anger is permanent and screenshot-able. A heated reply can turn one bad review into a story that spreads. A deleted-and-reposted exchange can destroy evidence. The professionals who clean these up fastest are the ones who slow down for the first ten minutes.

<Definition term="Fake review">
A Google review that does not reflect a genuine customer experience — fabricated by a non-customer, planted by a competitor or ex-employee, or used as a weapon rather than feedback. Fake reviews violate Google's content policies and are eligible for removal. A real customer's honest one-star review, however harsh, is not fake and cannot be removed.
</Definition>

So before you decide it's "fake," be precise. Some one-star reviews really are from upset real customers — and those follow a completely different path (a calm reply, not a report). The playbook below separates the two and tells you exactly what to do for each.

## The first 24 hours: your step-by-step checklist

<AnswerBox>
In the first 24 hours: (1) don't respond publicly, (2) screenshot the review and reviewer profile, (3) read it twice and de-escalate emotionally, (4) check it against the five Google policy categories, (5) search your customer records, (6) decide — report if it violates policy, reply calmly if it's a real customer, (7) report from your verified Business Profile with evidence, (8) keep your documentation in a safe place.
</AnswerBox>

Work through these in order. Each step protects either your evidence or your reputation.

1. **Stop. Don't post anything public yet.** Whatever you type in the first hour, you'll regret. Close the app if you have to.
2. **Screenshot the review.** Capture the full text, the star rating, the reviewer's display name, and the date — before the author can edit or delete it.
3. **Screenshot the reviewer's profile.** Click their name and capture their review history. A profile that one-stars businesses across the country in a day is a gift you'll want preserved.
4. **Read it twice, calmly.** Strip the emotion. Look only at the facts: Is there a real job described? A date? A crew name? Or is it vague and detail-free?
5. **Search your customer records.** Run the reviewer's name, phone, address, and email through your CRM, invoicing, and job calendar. No match is a major signal — and a dated screenshot of that empty search is evidence.
6. **Diagnose the policy category.** Does it fit fake engagement, conflict of interest, off-topic, spam, or prohibited content? Our full diagnostic in [how to tell if a Google review violates policy](/blog/how-to-tell-if-google-review-violates-policy) walks each category with contractor examples.
7. **Decide your path.** If it violates a policy and you can evidence it, report it. If it's a real customer's honest complaint, write a calm public reply instead.
8. **Back up your evidence.** Email the screenshots to yourself or drop them in cloud storage. Don't let your only record live on one phone.

<Callout type="tip">
Save the reviewer's profile screenshot even if you're not sure you'll report. Authors edit and delete reviews, and profiles change. The five minutes you spend documenting now is the difference between a winnable case and "it's your word against theirs" later.
</Callout>

## What NOT to do when you get a fake review

<AnswerBox>
Don't reply in anger, don't argue line-by-line in public, don't reveal the customer's private details, don't delete or ignore the review hoping it disappears, and don't pay anyone who demands money to take it down. The biggest self-inflicted damage from a fake review almost always comes from the contractor's reaction, not the review itself.
</AnswerBox>

These are the mistakes that turn a fixable problem into a lasting one:

- **Replying in anger.** A defensive, sarcastic, or hostile reply is read by every future customer. It makes you look like the problem, even when you're the victim.
- **Arguing point-by-point in public.** You won't win, and the back-and-forth keeps the review at the top of everyone's mind.
- **Posting private details.** Naming addresses, payment disputes, or personal information can violate privacy norms and makes you look vindictive.
- **Ignoring it and hoping.** Fake reviews don't evaporate. Silence lets it sit there scaring off homeowners while the trail goes cold.
- **Paying a "removal" demand.** If someone offers to take down a review for money, that's extortion — paying marks you as an easy target and rarely ends it.

<Callout type="warning">
If the review came with a demand — "pay me and I'll take it down," or "do this for free or it stays" — you're dealing with extortion, not a normal fake review. Stop, save every message, and do not pay. Extortion in writing is one of the strongest removal cases there is. Follow the specific steps in [removing extortion reviews and review threats](/blog/remove-extortion-review-threats).
</Callout>

## How do you tell a fake review from a real unhappy customer?

<AnswerBox>
A fake review usually lacks the details a real customer would know — no date, no crew name, no specific job — and often comes from a profile with no connection to you (no match in your records, scattershot reviews, or ties to a competitor). A real unhappy customer references an actual experience you can locate in your system. When in doubt, your own records settle it.
</AnswerBox>

This distinction decides everything, because the two get opposite treatment. Here's the quick comparison:

| Signal | Likely fake (report it) | Likely real customer (reply to it) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Customer record | No match in CRM, invoices, or calendar | Found — there's a job, invoice, or contract |
| Service details | Vague, generic, no specifics | References date, crew, or the actual work |
| Reviewer profile | Mass reviews, competitor ties, brand-new | Normal local profile, occasional reviews |
| Timing | Tied to a firing, lost bid, or refusal | Tied to an actual service you performed |
| Your move | Document and report the violation | Calm, professional public reply |

When the right column describes your review, the honest truth is that it's not removable — and trying to report an honest customer as fake can hurt your credibility with Google for future, valid reports. A real customer's harsh opinion is theirs to keep. The good news: a calm, fair public reply often does more for your reputation than a removal ever would, because future customers read how you handle criticism.

## How do you report a fake 1-star review?

<AnswerBox>
Report it from your verified Google Business Profile: open the review, select "Report review," choose the specific policy it violates, and submit. Then state your case in plain facts, track the report in your dashboard, and escalate with your evidence if there's no decision within about two weeks. Reporting from the owner account with clear evidence is what gets fast results.
</AnswerBox>

Once you've confirmed the review violates a policy, the reporting flow is straightforward:

1. **Log in to the account that manages your Business Profile.** Reports from the verified owner carry more weight than anonymous flags.
2. **Open the review and choose "Report review"** (sometimes a flag icon).
3. **Select the precise violation.** Fake engagement if they were never a customer; conflict of interest for a competitor or ex-employee; off-topic, spam, or prohibited content as it fits. Picking the wrong category is a top cause of denials.
4. **State the case in facts.** Lead with your strongest point — usually "this person is not in our customer records."
5. **Track and follow up.** No decision in two weeks? Escalate through Google Business Profile support and attach your evidence file.
