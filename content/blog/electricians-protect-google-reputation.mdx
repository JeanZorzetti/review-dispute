## Why is a bad review uniquely damaging for electricians?

<AnswerBox>
A bad review is uniquely damaging for electricians because the trade carries life-safety stakes that no other home service matches. When a review implies your work was unsafe — a panel that "sparked," wiring that "wasn't to code," an installation that "could have started a fire" — it attacks competence and safety, the exact things a homeowner cannot afford to gamble on. A homeowner might risk a mediocre paint job to save money, but almost no one will risk electrical work that might burn the house down. That's why a single believable safety-smear review can cost you multiple high-value panel and rewire jobs.
</AnswerBox>

Think about the difference in how customers evaluate trades. A homeowner reading reviews about a painter is weighing taste and tidiness; the worst case is they don't love the color. A homeowner reading reviews about you is weighing whether to trust you with the one system in their house that can kill someone or burn the place down. Electrical fires and electrocution aren't abstract fears — they're the first thing people associate with bad wiring. So when a review even hints that your work was dangerous, it lands on a nerve that's already raw.

<Definition term="Safety-smear review">
A review — often fabricated or posted by a non-customer or competitor — that frames an electrician's work as dangerous or non-compliant ("the outlet they installed shocked my kid," "the panel was a fire hazard," "not even to code"). Because electrical buyers fear exactly these outcomes, safety-smear reviews do disproportionate damage relative to their truth, and they're a common form of competitor sabotage in the trade.
</Definition>

This is also why your ticket size compounds the problem. Service-call work — a dead outlet, a tripping breaker — runs a couple hundred dollars. But the jobs that build an electrical business are the big ones: a panel upgrade at $2,000–$4,000, a whole-home rewire at $8,000–$15,000, a service upgrade or EV-charger-plus-panel package well into five figures. Those are deliberate, researched purchases, and a homeowner spending $10,000 to rewire a house they love will read every review twice. A fake one-star claiming your work was unsafe can quietly remove you from the running on the exact jobs that matter most.

## What kinds of fake and unfair reviews do electricians get?

<AnswerBox>
Electricians see a distinct set of fake and unfair reviews: fabricated "code violation" claims from people who were never customers, safety-smear reviews ("the panel sparked," "this was a fire hazard") used as competitor sabotage, license-questioning attacks ("this guy isn't even licensed"), permit-dispute spillover, and after-hours emergency pricing complaints. Each maps to a specific Google policy violation — and the strongest cases are backed by permits, inspection records, and your license documentation.
</AnswerBox>

These are the patterns that show up again and again on electrical profiles, and how each lines up with a removable violation.

- **Fabricated "code violation" claims.** Someone who was never your customer posts that your work "wasn't to code" or "failed inspection" — when in fact the job passed inspection or you never touched that property. If they were genuinely never your customer, this is **fake engagement** and highly removable, especially when your inspection sign-off says the opposite.
- **Safety-smear sabotage from competitors.** A rival posts as a customer claiming the panel you installed "sparked" or "could have burned the house down," often using insider terms a homeowner wouldn't ("they back-stabbed the outlets," "undersized the feeder," "no AFCI breakers"). When the profile also praises competing electricians and matches no one in your records, that's a **conflict-of-interest** violation.
- **License-questioning attacks.** "This guy isn't even a licensed electrician." When you hold a current, verifiable state license and the reviewer has no record of being your customer, that's a **false statement of fact** you can challenge with your license documentation.
- **Permit-dispute spillover.** A homeowner who didn't want to pay for a permit, or who got tangled in a permitting delay with the city, blames you in a review with false claims about the work. Honest frustration is protected; *fabricated* statements about the work or the permit are not.
- **Mistaken identity.** Electrical company names cluster around generic and city terms ("[City] Electric," "Bright Spark Electrical," "Mr. Electric"). A review meant for a different company lands on yours — an **off-topic** violation.

<Callout type="warning">
Not every critical electrical review is removable. A homeowner who writes "they left the wall open for a week and never patched the drywall they cut" is describing a real experience — even at one star, that's honest feedback Google protects. Report it as fake and you burn credibility you'll need for real cases. The right move is a calm, specific public reply. </Callout>

## What evidence gets a fake electrical review removed?

<AnswerBox>
Removals are won with electrician-specific documentation that proves either "this person was never our customer" or "this reviewer has a conflict of interest" or "this factual claim is false." The strongest electrical evidence is a no-match search of your job records, pulled permits tied to the property address, inspection pass records that contradict a "code violation" claim, your current state license documentation, and dated photos of the panel or finished work. Lead with the single clearest fact — usually the inspection sign-off or the empty records search.
</AnswerBox>

Electricians have an unusual advantage in removal cases: your trade is heavily documented by third parties. Permits and inspections are public records created by the city, not by you — which makes them powerful, neutral evidence that's hard for anyone to wave away.

| Evidence type | Why it's powerful for electricians |
| --- | --- |
| **Inspection pass records** | A city inspector signing off on the exact work a review calls a "code violation" is neutral, third-party proof the claim is false |
| **Pulled permits** | Electrical permits are public and tied to addresses — they show whether you ever worked at the property the review claims |
| **Current state license** | Directly rebuts "this guy isn't licensed" claims with verifiable, official documentation |
| **No-match job-records search** | A dated screenshot showing the reviewer's name, address, and phone return zero matches anchors any non-customer case |
| **Dated panel / finished-work photos** | Time-stamped photos of clean, labeled panel work counter fabricated "fire hazard" and "sloppy wiring" claims |
| **Reviewer profile history** | A profile praising competing electricians and only attacking you signals competitor sabotage |

The filing process itself is the same one every contractor uses — choose the precise violation, attach your evidence, report from your verified owner account, and escalate if denied. The complete step-by-step lives in [how to remove a fake Google review](/blog/how-to-remove-fake-google-review). The electrician-specific part is *which records you bring* — and the trade that documents permits, inspections, and licensing well has a real edge, because those records come from the city, not from you.

<Callout type="tip">
Your compliance paperwork is also your reputation defense. Electricians who file every permit, keep the inspection sign-off, photograph the finished panel with labeled breakers, and store the license number on every job can build a removal case in minutes. When a safety-smear review hits, the inspector's signature is your single strongest counter-fact — keep it where you can find it fast.
</Callout>
