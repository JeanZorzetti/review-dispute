## Do Google reviews affect local ranking?

<AnswerBox>
Yes — Google reviews directly affect local ranking. They're one of the strongest inputs to the "prominence" component of Google's local search algorithm, which determines where your business shows up in local results and the map pack. Review count, average rating, recency, the keywords customers use, and your response rate all factor in. Reviews aren't the only thing that matters — relevance and distance also count — but among the signals you can actively control, reviews are the most powerful.
</AnswerBox>

Google has been unusually clear about this. Its own guidance for local businesses states that local results are based on three core factors — relevance, distance, and prominence — and that "review count and review score factor into local search ranking," with more reviews and higher ratings improving a business's local position. So the question isn't *whether* reviews matter for ranking; Google says they do. The useful questions are *which* aspects of your reviews matter and how to act on them.

<Definition term="Local ranking">
The order in which businesses appear in Google's geographically targeted search results — including the map pack and the local Maps listings — when someone searches for a service near them. Local ranking is determined by Google's local algorithm, which weighs relevance (how well you match the search), distance (how close you are to the searcher), and prominence (how established and well-regarded your business is, where reviews play a major role).
</Definition>

The mistake contractors make is assuming ranking is fixed or rigged. It isn't. It's a scoreboard you can move — and reviews are the lever with the longest handle. Before getting into the specific signals, it helps to see where reviews sit among everything Google weighs.

## The three pillars of local ranking, and where reviews fit

<AnswerBox>
Google's local algorithm rests on three factors: relevance (how well your business matches what the person searched), distance (how close you are to the searcher), and prominence (how well-known and established your business is). Reviews live mostly inside prominence — they signal that real people use and rate your business — but they also touch relevance, because the keywords in reviews help Google understand what services you actually provide.
</AnswerBox>

Understanding this split keeps you honest about what reviews can and can't do:

| Ranking factor | What it means | Can you control it? | Role of reviews |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Relevance** | How well your profile matches the search | Partly — via categories, services, profile content | Keywords in reviews reinforce what you do |
| **Distance** | How close you are to the searcher | Barely — it's based on location | None directly |
| **Prominence** | How established and trusted you are | Yes — heavily | Major: count, rating, recency, response rate |

The takeaway: you can't make yourself closer to a searcher, and you can only partly tune relevance. But prominence is wide open, and reviews are the biggest piece of it you control. That's why review strategy is the highest-leverage local SEO work most contractors can do. For the deeper mechanics of how these factors combine to choose the businesses Google features in the map block, see [reviews and the local pack](/blog/reviews-and-the-local-pack).

## The review signals that actually matter for ranking

<AnswerBox>
Five review signals carry the most weight for local ranking: review count (more genuine reviews signal prominence), average rating (higher is better, with a sweet spot in the 4.2–4.9 range), recency (a steady flow of fresh reviews beats an old pile), keywords (natural service mentions add relevance), and response rate (replying signals an active business). No single one wins on its own — Google reads them together as a picture of how trustworthy and established your business is.
</AnswerBox>

Here's how each signal works and what to do with it.

### Review count

Volume is foundational. More genuine reviews tell Google your business is real, active, and used by many people. There's no universal threshold — what matters is your count relative to the competitors chasing the same searches. A profile with 150 reviews carries more prominence than one with 12, all else equal, and it's also far more resilient: a single bad review barely moves a large average. The action is simple and unglamorous — earn reviews relentlessly, from every satisfied customer, forever.

### Average rating

Rating works alongside count. A high average reinforces prominence and, just as importantly, drives the click once you're ranked. Interestingly, a flawless 5.0 can read as suspicious to both Google and shoppers; a believable average in the low-to-high 4s often converts best. The action is to keep your average high through volume and quality, not by trying to scrub honest criticism.

### Recency

A stack of reviews from three years ago tells Google your business *was* active. A steady drip of new reviews tells it you *are*. Recency is one of the most underrated signals — a profile earning a few reviews every month outranks a stale one with a higher total. The action: never let your review flow stall. Build the request into every job close so fresh reviews keep arriving.

### Keywords in reviews

When customers naturally describe what you did — "they fixed our emergency AC failure in July," "great roof leak repair" — those words attach service relevance to your profile. You can't and must never script reviews, but high volume surfaces this language organically, reinforcing what you're known for. The action: simply earning lots of genuine reviews tends to produce keyword-rich text on its own.

### Response rate

Replying to reviews signals an engaged, accountable business — exactly the prominence Google rewards — and Google has publicly encouraged it. Responses also keep your profile fresh and can naturally carry relevant terms. The action: reply to every review, positive and negative. It helps ranking a little and conversions a lot.

<Callout type="tip">
If you can only do one thing for your local ranking this quarter, make it earning more recent reviews. Count and recency are the two signals with the most headroom for most contractors, and they're entirely within your control. A one-tap review request added to your job-close checklist moves both at once.
</Callout>

## How reviews connect to the local pack and the map

<AnswerBox>
The local pack is the block of three business listings with a map that appears at the top of local searches — and it captures the majority of clicks for "near me" and service-area searches. Reviews heavily influence which three businesses Google features there, because the pack is driven by the same relevance-distance-prominence formula, and reviews are the largest controllable part of prominence. Winning the pack is largely a function of out-reviewing your local competitors.
</AnswerBox>

For a contractor, the local pack is the prize. It sits above the regular blue-link results, it's where homeowners click first, and it's where most local leads come from. Because the pack shows only three businesses, the competition is fierce and the review signals that decide it are decisive. A contractor with strong count, a high average, fresh reviews, and consistent responses is the one Google tends to surface — and being surfaced is what turns reputation into phone calls. ## What reviews can't do for ranking — and common myths

<AnswerBox>
Reviews can't override distance, instantly rank a brand-new profile, or compensate for a thin, incomplete Business Profile. They're powerful but not magic. Buying fake reviews to game ranking backfires — it violates Google's policy and risks penalties — and a single review, good or bad, rarely changes anything. Reviews work as a sustained signal over time, not a switch you flip.
</AnswerBox>

A few myths worth killing:

- **"More reviews instantly rank me."** Reviews are a sustained signal. A burst of new ones helps over time; it doesn't flip your rank overnight.
- **"A bad review will tank my ranking."** Against a healthy backdrop of recent positives, a stray one-star barely registers. The real ranking risks are a low average, stalled flow, or a thin profile.
- **"Buying reviews is a shortcut."** It's a policy violation that can get your profile penalized or suspended — far more damaging than the slow, honest route.
- **"Reviews are all that matter."** They're the biggest controllable factor, but a neglected Business Profile, wrong categories, or missing service-area settings will hold you back no matter how many reviews you have.

When your reviews are strong but you're still not ranking, the cause is usually one of these profile or technical issues, all of which are diagnosable. The full checklist is in [why your Google Business Profile isn't ranking](/blog/why-google-business-profile-not-ranking).

<Callout type="warning">
A fake one-star from a competitor or non-customer is a legitimate ranking and conversion problem, but the answer is targeted removal of the policy violation — not drowning it in fake positives. Buying reviews to compensate is a policy violation in its own right and risks far more than the single bad review ever could. Earn real reviews; remove only the ones that break the rules.
</Callout>

## Turning the ranking signals into a routine

Knowing the signals is useless without a habit that feeds them. Here's the routine that keeps all five working for you:

1. **Earn reviews every job.** A one-tap request at job close drives count and recency — the two highest-leverage signals — simultaneously.
2. **Respond to everything.** Reply to every review within days, in a calm professional tone, naturally referencing the service. This nudges response rate, keywords, and conversions at once.
3. **Keep the profile complete.** Accurate categories, services, hours, service areas, and photos make sure your strong reviews actually translate into relevance and visibility.
4. **Benchmark against competitors quarterly.** Check the review count and average of the businesses winning your local searches. Ranking is relative — your goal is to out-earn them.
5. **Remove only genuine violations.** If a fake or competitor review is dragging your average, address that specific policy violation rather than gaming the system with fake positives.
